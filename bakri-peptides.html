Reference Guide

Read this first — how to use this guide

Most of what follows is unproven, unregulated, or both.

Dr. Bakri is a board-certified internal medicine physician and is consistently careful: throughout the conversation he flags missing human data, repeats "this is not medical advice," and stresses that most of these compounds should only be used under physician supervision. This guide preserves his framework as given. But the framework should be read with three things in mind.

The evidence base is thin. With the exception of the GLP-1 agonists and a small number of approved indications, the compounds here are not FDA-approved. BPC-157 has essentially one small human trial; much of the bioregulator (epitalon, pinealon, thymalin) evidence rests on non-replicated Soviet-era Russian literature; the longevity and DNA-repair claims are extraordinary and unverified. Animal data and anecdote are not the same as human safety and efficacy data.

The supply chain is unreliable. Most peptides are bought through "research use only" gray-market sites of unknown purity and contents. People have injected the wrong compound entirely. Quality varies batch to batch even within the legitimate compounding market.

Some protocols described here are dangerous if copied. Dr. Bakri describes self-experiments — high-dose self-injection of BPC-157, taking quadruple the starting dose of a GLP-1 — that he explicitly tells listeners not to repeat. Where he says "don't do this," this guide flags it again. Nothing here is medical advice. Decisions about any of these compounds belong with a qualified clinician.

01

What Peptides Actually Are

"Peptides" has become a marketing word for things people buy and inject. The biology underneath is simpler — and the most useful first distinction is whether a peptide has a known receptor or not, because that largely predicts how predictable its effects will be.

A peptide is a short chain of amino acids — one of the body's signalling "languages," sitting between proteins (long chains) and steroid hormones (a different language entirely). The path runs DNA → RNA → proteins, and proteins can be broken down into polypeptides and peptides. Bakri's organising frame is to split the whole category in two.

RECEPTORPeptides with known receptors — e.g. the GLP-1 agonists. They bind a defined target, so their clinical effects are strong and relatively predictable. NO RECEPTORPeptides with no clearly identified receptor — e.g. BPC-157, TB-500/TB-4. Effects are real in animal models but the mechanism and target are obscure.

For the no-receptor group, several mechanisms are plausible: the receptor may simply be undiscovered; the peptide may modify or link existing proteins into more favourable configurations; or, as proposed for the Russian "bioregulator" peptides, it may act epigenetically — binding into the groove of DNA at specific sites to open or close chromatin and shift which genes are expressed. That last mode is conceptually closer to how a steroid hormone works (testosterone or DHT entering the nucleus and switching on a programme, as in puberty) than to a classic drug-receptor interaction.

A recurring theme: nearly every organ seems to carry its own "signature" peptides, and the historical bet has been that gut peptides have gut effects, muscle peptides have muscle effects, and so on. The 19th-century discovery of carnosine and carnitine in muscle tissue is an early example of that logic.

There's no scientific category of "peptide" that gives you a functional definition two people can usefully discuss. Do you mean carnosine, or do you mean retatrutide? — Dr. Abud Bakri (paraphrased)
Bakri's broader point: the word "peptide" is too general to carry meaning. What matters is not whether something is technically a peptide, but which receptor it touches. An oral GLP-1 drug that is not a peptide is far more similar to semaglutide than BPC-157 is — so "peptides are good / bad" is a category error.
02

BPC-157: Where It Came From

BPC-157 has a longer and stranger history than its gym reputation suggests. It begins not with bodybuilding but with a search for a gut-protective compound in gastric juice — and a 1990s Croatian lab that still sits at the centre of almost all the evidence.

The arc runs through three figures. Pavlov — famous for conditioning experiments — also worked on dog gastric juices, collecting them through a surgical opening in the stomach and selling them as a medicinal "elixir" for digestive complaints. Hans Selye, developing his stress-adaptation theory, documented that stressed animals showed three changes: enlarged adrenals (more cortisol), a destroyed gastric lining, and a shrunken thymus and lymphatic system. Put together, these suggested the gut might contain its own cytoprotective compound — something that defends the stomach lining against damage.

In 1991, a group in Croatia isolated a large (~40 kDa) protein from gastric juice and named it BPC — Body Protection Compound. BPC-157 is a 15-amino-acid fragment of that larger protein, identified as the segment driving the biological effects. A crucial and frequently misstated point:

The human body does not naturally make "BPC-157." It is a synthetic fragment of a larger gastric protein. The common online claim that it is a substance your body already produces is inaccurate.

The animal work that followed is what built the reputation. Researchers induced injuries in mice — severed tendons and ACLs, burn wounds — and reported faster healing with BPC given orally or intraperitoneally. Burn-injured animals that normally develop severe gastric ulcers developed none when given BPC. The Achilles tendon study in particular is what ignited bodybuilder interest and redirected attention toward musculoskeletal use — even though the original purpose was always gastric protection, not sports injury.

03

BPC-157: How It Might Work

The puzzle: a gut peptide with no known receptor appears to accelerate repair across radically different tissue types — tendon, ligament, even nerve. Nerves, especially in the central nervous system, are notoriously reluctant to regenerate, which is what makes the claims striking.

Rather than acting through one target, BPC-157 appears to push multiple growth and repair pathways at once:

  • Angiogenesis (VEGF): increased vascular endothelial growth factor signalling — more blood vessel formation at the injury site. This is also the source of its biggest safety question (see Section 04).
  • Cell migration: enhanced movement of repair cells to the area, reportedly stronger when combined with TB-500/TB-4.
  • Growth hormone receptors: in one tendon model, BPC increased growth hormone receptors on the tendon, theoretically letting more GH dock in and drive regrowth.
  • Nitric oxide: modulation of NO synthesis, dilating vessels and recruiting cells — central to wound healing.
  • Anti-stress / pro-repair under cortisol: normally corticosteroids stall wound healing; with BPC present, healing was preserved or improved.

An important clarification on inflammation: BPC does not appear to simply suppress it. Blocking inflammation outright (as corticosteroids do) removes the signals that call in tissue-repair machinery. BPC instead seems to keep the foot on the accelerator of the regenerative response — bringing in immune cells and healing factors — rather than damping the whole system down.

The neurological and gut–brain effects (the more interesting half)

In rodent studies, BPC blunted intoxication (animals "got less drunk") and prevented the symptoms of alcohol withdrawal — which in humans can be fatal without benzodiazepines. Bakri reads this as a clue that BPC's most interesting effects may be neurological and gut–brain rather than musculoskeletal.

In humans, the anecdotal signals point the same way. Some users report their stimulant medication (e.g. Adderall) feels blunted on oral BPC; online communities describe anhedonia, low mood and low energy. Bakri's interpretation is that BPC acts homeostatically on dopaminergic signalling through the gut–brain axis — preventing the brain from going "too far" in either direction. The proposed teleology: if BPC is a body-protection compound, it may shift the body toward a "rest-and-digest," convalescent state to heal — which would also explain why it removes both stimulant highs and withdrawal lows.

Bakri and Huberman repeatedly stress these are speculative mechanisms layered on anecdote. The honest summary is that the gut–brain effects are intriguing and poorly understood, not established.
04

BPC-157: Safety, Dosing & the One-Lab Problem

This is where the gap between enthusiasm and evidence is widest. The animal safety record looks clean — but almost all of it comes from a single research group, the human data amount to essentially one trial, and the most worrying theoretical risk has never been properly tested.

What the data do and don't show

  • No established LD50. Animals have tolerated doses ~1,000× typical with no obvious harm, so the lethal dose is unknown — which itself blocks normal FDA-style drug development.
  • One human trial of substance. Small Phase 1 / Phase 2 trials of rectal BPC enemas (up to ~80 mg) for ulcerative colitis in the early 2000s, from the same Croatian group. Phase 1 showed no adverse effects; the small Phase 2 (~40 patients) showed a positive signal. Full data were never published — only abstracts.
  • Orally/rectally dosed BPC may not go systemic. When measured, BPC-157 was not detectable in blood — either broken down rapidly or staying local to the gut lining. Injected BPC does reach the bloodstream.
  • Almost all animal data come from one lab. A few Chinese groups have replicated parts; a US Phase 2 trial (hamstrings) is reportedly starting. But the single-source problem is the central caveat on everything above.

The angiogenesis / cancer question

The mechanistic concern: if BPC promotes blood-vessel growth (VEGF), it could in theory vascularise an existing or nascent tumour and accelerate it. Bakri's position is that carcinogenic compounds usually leave a signature in animal data and BPC shows none — and in at least one melanoma cell-line model it actually decreased VEGF, so it is not a uniform angiogenesis promoter. But "no signal" again rests largely on one lab, and there are anecdotal reports of worsening hematomas and of facial spider-angiomas getting worse on BPC, which is consistent with an angiogenic effect.

Anyone with a known or suspected cancer, or significant cancer risk, has a clear theoretical reason for caution with BPC-157. The honest state of knowledge is "we don't know" — not "proven safe."

Dosing, anecdotes, and a self-experiment

There is no established human dose. Online protocols cluster around 200–250 micrograms simply because that is what fits a vial and what peptide websites suggest — not because of human data. Bakri argues the effective human dose is probably far higher than the mouse-translated microgram figures.

His own account: after tearing his tricep (a grade-two tear, arm purple top to bottom, typically a ~3-month recovery), he injected BPC-157 locally alongside other peptides and reported being back to normal in 3–4 weeks. Huberman separately describes a ~200 microgram injection into a badly pulled trap that resolved in two days. Both stress these are uncontrolled anecdotes — the natural-healing counterfactual is unknown, and the placebo effect is real.

Dr. Bakri explicitly says: don't do this at home; this is not medical advice. For the tricep, he injected doses "up in the grams" — orders of magnitude above the microgram range in the literature — of a non-approved peptide with one human trial. Self-injecting unregulated compounds at self-chosen supraphysiological doses is exactly the practice the framing note at the top warns against. It is recounted here as his experience, not as a protocol to follow.

The balanced read, in Bakri's own framing: either BPC is as good as enthusiasts claim and it is a tragedy that rigorous data don't exist — or it is ineffective or harmful and millions are injecting it blindly. Both are bad outcomes, and only proper trials can tell them apart.

05

Sourcing, Legality & Quality Control

For most listeners this is the most practically important — and most fraught — part of the conversation. Where peptides come from, and whether the vial contains what the label claims, is largely unregulated.

PHARMAStandard "big pharma" product — most stringent quality control. Applies to approved GLP-1s (pens) and a handful of approved peptides. COMPOUNDINGCompounding pharmacies — quality varies. The best do full sterility and purity testing; others are weak. Often must add a B12/B6 to meet compounding rules. GRAY MARKET"Research use only" websites — how the majority of people obtain peptides. Unreliable; contents unknown; can be excellent, useless, or the wrong compound. Varies batch to batch. BLACK MARKETBuying raw vials directly from overseas suppliers, or "bathtub" synthesis. Cheapest and most dangerous — contents entirely unknown.

A structural fact behind all of this: the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) for essentially every peptide — semaglutide, tirzepatide, BPC and the rest — is synthesised in China. There is no meaningful US-made peptide supply yet. The raw or finished compound is then packaged or finished domestically. So the difference between "pharma" and "gray market" is less about origin and more about the testing and quality control applied downstream.

A real cautionary case discussed: a person injecting what they believed was retatrutide began tanning unexpectedly — they had actually been injecting melanotan, a different peptide entirely. This is the concrete danger of unverified sources: you may not be taking what you think you are taking.

The shifting legal status (US)

BPC-157 was never FDA-approved, so it lives on compounding lists. The FDA uses category 1 (acceptable to compound), category 2 (do not compound), and category 3. From roughly 2017–2024 BPC was prescribed in anti-aging/alternative practices; in late 2024 it and ~20 other peptides were moved to category 2 ("do not compound"). As of around April of the discussion year it was removed from category 2 but not yet placed on category 1 — leaving it in limbo.

In the meantime, compounders relabel it as PDA — pentadecapeptide arginate — which is, functionally, the same compound (an arginate vs acetate salt). State medical boards view all of this very differently from the FDA: some explicitly prohibit prescribing any non-FDA-approved peptide regardless of FDA stance. For telehealth, the law of the patient's state governs. Net effect: a genuinely patchwork, state-by-state, "wild west" picture.

Why the GLP-1s normalised all of this

Bakri's read on why peptides exploded: the GLP-1 wave made millions of ordinary people comfortable injecting themselves. Five years ago the idea of a self-injection was alarming; now it is routine. BPC and the older peptides effectively "rode in" on that cultural shift. He also notes that more than half the peptide market is female — driven heavily by skin/collagen interest (GHK-Cu) and by affiliate-style selling.

Pinealon (EDR): Sleep & Cognition

Pinealon belongs to the Russian "bioregulator" family discovered by Dr. Vladimir Khavinson — Soviet-era research aimed at protecting soldiers, pilots and astronauts from accelerated aging. It is a tripeptide (sequence Glu-Asp-Arg, "EDR") with no known receptor.

SOURCEDespite the name, pinealon is not from the pineal gland — it derives from a ground-up cortical brain extract (cortexin). (It is epitalon that comes from the pineal — see Section 07.) MECHANISMNo receptor. Proposed to act like a partial transcription factor — binding the DNA groove at promoter regions to expose them for transcription, modulating pathways such as PPAR-alpha/gamma, SOD1/2, GDF11. PURPOSEKhavinson developed it as an anti-stress, cognitive-performance compound. In exhausted athletes, the pinealon group maintained performance better than placebo.

The most striking anecdote comes from Huberman himself: taken at the start of the night it reduced his deep slow-wave sleep and over-amplified REM (an unwanted effect). Taken instead as a small injection on a middle-of-the-night waking, it raised his REM fraction substantially — and seemed to lift REM on subsequent nights too, consistent with the "bioregulator" idea that benefits accrue and linger after dosing rather than tracking each dose. Notably, Khavinson's own literature never mentions REM sleep at all (1970s Soviet labs had no sleep trackers), so this is an unstudied observation.

Bakri's framing is that it is better used in the morning or pre-workout: users report less brain fog, clearer thinking and better high-intensity performance, attributed to neurons being kept in a better oxidative state. The dreaming/REM effect is something he'd reposition as a side effect rather than the point.

ORAL DOSE~0.5–3 mg is where users settle (orally available via Lat peptide transporters). RUSSIAN DOSEKhavinson-origin products ~200 micrograms; some people inject (goes systemic, likely crosses the blood–brain barrier).
Two cautions Bakri raises directly: pinealon can drop blood sugar (via PPAR-alpha/gamma activation) — relevant for anyone hypoglycaemic or with blood-sugar issues — and it can produce very vivid dreams or nightmares, which some people find distressing. Risk data overall are limited and come from Russian literature only.
07

Epitalon & the Pineal Gland

Epitalon (a tetrapeptide, "AEDG") is the pineal-derived member of the bioregulator family — and the one carrying the boldest longevity and DNA-repair claims. It is also where the strongest reader caution about evidence quality applies.

Khavinson's pineal-extract drug (epithalamin) restored circadian rhythmicity and melatonin output. In aged monkeys — whose melatonin has declined sharply since puberty — it reportedly restored melatonin production, with no effect in young animals (i.e. it appeared to restore rather than push past normal). Epitalon is the purified peptide doing much of that work. It is proposed to up-regulate clock-gene expression and the enzyme converting serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → melatonin.

What drew Huberman in was the eye/vision angle: papers (in mice) using epitalon against neurodegeneration in models of retinitis pigmentosa and glaucoma — conditions where photoreceptors and optic-nerve machinery are progressively lost. The appeal is that it appears to act at the level of genetic/DNA-repair machinery rather than yet another vague downstream receptor — conceptually closer to gene therapy as "support for genetic machinery." Bakri's speculation is that it may up-regulate melanopsin, which would make morning sunlight more effective.

This is the strongest "extraordinary claim" territory in the episode. The headline longevity result — Khavinson's 15-year nursing-home study reporting that epithalamin + thymalin, given only ~10–20 days a year, lowered cardiovascular, infectious and cancer mortality — is a remarkable claim resting on non-replicated Soviet-era data. Both speakers explicitly flag the "Russian literature, big caveat" problem. Telomerase/anti-aging claims for epitalon are heavily promoted commercially and are not supported by rigorous human evidence.

Does the pineal calcify with age?

A genuinely open question. Calcification is visible on some MRIs (e.g. in stroke/TBI patients), but whether it drives the decline in melatonin is unclear. Khavinson's view is that the age-related loss of pineal function is more physiological than anatomical — a deterioration in melatonin synthesis rather than simply a calcified gland. Pineal research is under-developed partly because the gland has a "woo" reputation that deters serious neuroscientists.

08

The Thymus & Thymic Involution

The thymus is the gland behind much of the immune-peptide interest. Understanding its lifecycle — why it grows, why it shrinks, and what that costs us — frames everything in Section 09.

The thymus sits just above the heart, behind the collarbone. It is both a hormone-secreting gland and the "training school" where T-cells (made in bone marrow) learn not to attack the body's own tissue. It is large in infancy and childhood, then begins to shrink — thymic involution — from puberty onward, mostly under the influence of androgens, estrogens, progesterone and corticosteroids. By adulthood it is largely fat with small globules of thymic tissue.

YOUTHAt ~15, the body makes enormous numbers of naïve T-cells daily, which mature into CD4/CD8 cells living 10–15 years. AGINGOutput falls steeply; as it hits a floor, rates of cancer, heart disease and autoimmunity rise. A 2026 Nature paper linked higher "thymic score" on MRI to lower mortality across multiple conditions. PREGNANCYThe thymus involutes during pregnancy (avoiding immune attack on the fetus) then regrows during breastfeeding under growth hormone and prolactin. Hibernating animals show the same shrink/regrow cycle.
A sobering historical note used to illustrate medical fallibility: in the 1910s–1940s, doctors irradiated the thymuses of children — believing an "oversized" thymus caused sudden infant death — as gold-standard practice. Thousands of those children later died of cancers. Separately, removing residual thymic tissue during open-heart surgery (a common practice) is associated with a mortality signal — higher cancer and autoimmune rates — within five years (New England Journal of Medicine). The lesson Bakri draws: be humble and cautious about interventions, including the ones discussed here.

Can you maintain or regrow the thymus? Dr. Greg Fahy's TRIIM trial gave a cocktail of growth hormone, metformin and DHEA for 12 months and reported increased thymic size on imaging, improved CD4/CD8 counts and ratio, and reduced markers of T-cell exhaustion (e.g. PD-1) — using growth hormone to drive thymic regrowth. Bakri also links thymic atrophy after infection (the thymus shrinks after every infection and regrows in recovery) to long-COVID-type pictures, arguing that modern life — chronic stress, poor light, processed food — denies people the convalescent recovery window the thymus needs.

09

Thymic Peptides: Thymosin α1, TB-500 & Thymulin

Three different molecules get tangled together online. They share a thymic origin but do quite different things — and only one was ever formally approved anywhere.

Thymosin alpha-1 (Zadaxin)

A ≥21-amino-acid thymic hormone from the Goldstein lab. It increases T-cell development and proliferation and makes T-cells more effective against pathogens — "jet fuel for T-cells," pro-immune. It was FDA-approved as Zadaxin for children born without a functional thymus (e.g. DiGeorge syndrome); that approval is no longer thought to exist in the US, though it is approved elsewhere as an adjuvant for hepatitis B/C and some cancers. Sepsis/infection data are not especially promising. Bakri uses it himself prophylactically when travelling or working hospital wards (≈2.5 mg twice weekly) and reports not getting sick — explicitly an N-of-1, not FDA-sanctioned use.

Thymosin beta-4 / TB-500

A 43-amino-acid peptide (also from the Goldstein lab) involved in the actin cytoskeleton — helping cells, including immune cells, reorganise and move quickly. This is the basis for its role in cell migration and repair (and why it is paired with BPC). It is a well-known doping agent in horse racing. TB-500 is a commonly used name for the synthetic version.

Thymulin (not thymalin) — the zinc-dependent one

A 9-amino-acid peptide (the French-identified main thymic hormone) that carries a zinc ion to do its work — biology pairing metals with peptides, as iron does in hemoglobin and copper in GHK. It is a marker of thymic function, declines sharply with age, and is zinc-dependent: falling thymulin is the earliest sign of zinc depletion, before serum or RBC zinc drop. In animal models it sensitises end-organs to hormone production — e.g. more testosterone from hCG when thymulin is present. Because its half-life is very short, the practical aim is to raise endogenous thymulin, which requires adequate zinc status, rather than to inject it.

Naming trap Bakri flags repeatedly: thymulin (zinc peptide, France) is constantly confused with thymalin (the Russian polypeptide thymic mix) on peptide websites. They are different.

Growth Hormone Secretagogues & Somatopause

These compounds don't supply growth hormone — they press the body's own GH "gas pedal." The appeal is reversing the GH decline of aging; the debate is whether doing so is wise.

Somatopause is the steep fall in growth hormone production that begins around the 30s — one of a sequence (pineal decline before puberty, thymic involution after, somatopause in the 30s, then adrenopause/menopause/andropause). The question driving secretagogue use: should declining IGF-1 be replenished? Proponents point to GH's real benefits for skin, sleep quality, muscle recovery, joints — and its thymus-regenerative effect (Fahy's work).

TESAMORELINA GHRH analogue; FDA-approved (for HIV-associated lipodystrophy). Higher fidelity, fewer "off-target" ghrelin effects. Strong GH/IGF-1 response, especially stacked with ipamorelin. SERMORELINA weaker GHRH-type secretagogue. IPAMORELINA GHRP; not FDA-approved. Combined with tesamorelin can push IGF-1 to puberty-level highs (380s–390s). MK-677An orally available ghrelin agonist — not a peptide. Causes a large, non-pulsatile GH release and the most "off-target" effects (hunger, prolactin, cortisol). GH is normally pulsatile (released in early slow-wave sleep).
The core risks Bakri raises: GH and secretagogues worsen insulin sensitivity (A1C tends to rise — the bodybuilding joke is "you have to be lean and healthy enough to take growth hormone"); and while there is no evidence GH causes cancer, it is a growth factor that could in theory accelerate an existing one. Animal and cross-breed evidence (higher-IGF-1 dog breeds live shorter lives; GH-deficient models live longer) feeds a genuine longevity debate — antagonistic pleiotropy: good in youth, possibly costly for lifespan. He suggests any use should be cyclical and clinician-monitored, not year-round.

Huberman's own self-experiment with sermorelin: it dramatically increased deep sleep but suppressed REM ("the opposite of pinealon"), and — the reason he stopped almost immediately — it spiked his PSA (prostate-specific antigen) from a low baseline. Off it, the PSA reverted. A reminder that GH-axis manipulation interacts with prostate growth (BPH, and the DHT/estrogen/GH drivers of prostate enlargement with age).

The "trinity stack" / celebrity protocol

The combination behind many rapid CEO/celebrity body transformations: GLP-1 (as the insulin-sensitivity / fat-loss tool) + a growth hormone modulator (GH itself if affordable, otherwise a secretagogue like ipamorelin) + androgen modulation (TRT, sometimes with an oral anabolic). Large fat loss and muscle gain in short windows. Whether it is healthy, Bakri's verdict is an open "we'll find out."

This is a stack of mostly non-approved or off-label hormonal agents used cosmetically. The insulin, cancer-growth and prostate concerns above apply, compounded by the unknowns of combining them. Presented as a description of what people do — not a recommendation.

GHK-Cu: The Copper Peptide

The peptide that drives much of the (largely female) skincare interest. It has the best topical human evidence of the "regenerative" peptides — and the weakest case for the injectable hype.

GHK-Cu is a tripeptide — glycine-histidine-lysine — carrying a copper ion, found within type-1 collagen fibres (skin, hair, connective tissue). Discovered by Dr. Lauren Pickart in the mid-1970s, it is high in youth (~200 ng/mL range) and falls steeply with age (down to the 60s by age ~65), tracking the loss of youthful skin. It both builds and breaks down collagen — and that dual action is the point: pure synthesis or pure breakdown produces poor tissue structure, whereas proper remodelling needs both.

SKIN (TOPICAL)The strongest evidence. Pickart's work compared it favourably to retinol and vitamin C; anecdotal crow's-feet improvement. Best used with a skincare routine, not instead of it. Genuine human data exist here. + RED LIGHTThe blue copper pigment and red-light therapy appear synergistic; some evidence for post-UV photo-damage repair. HAIRA hair study was not promising. Not a minoxidil replacement — at best a possible adjunct. LUNGA Chinese group is studying GHK-Cu for lung/connective-tissue regeneration (COPD, smokers) — early-stage hype, not established.

A practical quality tell: real GHK-Cu is blue (from the copper). But blue alone doesn't prove authenticity — it could be copper that has fallen out of the complex. A well-formulated topical from a competent skincare brand matters, because poorly formulated versions break down or don't penetrate. Online, GHK-Cu is bundled with BPC and TB-500 into marketing names like the "Wolverine stack" / "glow stack" — terms invented by sellers, not science. There's also debate about whether mixing these peptides denatures them.

Bakri is uneasy about non-experts injecting GHK-Cu into the face. His clear preference: a well-formulated topical, ideally paired with sunlight or red light — not facial self-injection. (And the real source of the helpful long-wavelength light is sunlight, with care to avoid excess UV.)

GLP-1s: Semaglutide to Retatrutide

The one category here with strong receptor pharmacology and real trial data — and the reason the whole peptide world took off. These are not in the "unproven" bucket the way the others are, though dosing and self-experimentation still carry real risk.

SEMAGLUTIDEOzempic (diabetes) / Wegovy (FDA-approved for weight loss). A GLP-1 agonist. TIRZEPATIDEMounjaro / Zepbound (the weight-loss-approved label). Dual GLP-1 + GIP. RETATRUTIDENewest; hits GLP-1, GIP and glucagon pathways. Generally grouped under "GLP-1" though it is broader. Reportedly fewer GI side effects. ORAL OPTIONAn oral GLP-1 agonist exists that is not a peptide — underscoring that the receptor matters, not the "peptide" label.

Origin: GLP-1 was discovered in Gila monster saliva; native GLP-1 is too short-acting, so pharma engineered longer-lasting versions — a genuine pharma success. The diabetes benefit (less insulin need, weight loss, more energy) was seen first, then translated to obesity. Bakri's macro view: facing an obesity/pre-diabetes epidemic the system couldn't otherwise afford to treat, GLP-1s are cautiously transformative — getting 10–30% body-weight reductions where older drugs got 5–10%.

Mechanistically, weight is a daily calculation the brain makes from many gut and hormonal signals (GLP, GIP, glucagon, insulin, leptin, sex hormones) integrating toward a set point. A GLP-1 is a large "don't eat" signal. Stop the drug and many — not all — regain weight, because the underlying set point hasn't changed. Whether people must stay on indefinitely, and how to taper, is poorly guided by the manufacturers.

Brain, mood and fertility effects

Modern GLP-1 drugs raise GLP signalling far above the diabetic-drug range — and the long-term effects on neuroplasticity and learning are unknown (could be positive or negative). The reported "lowered drive / low mood" may be partly the drug and partly confounders: low blood pressure, inadequate electrolytes, too little food and lost social eating when people take excessive doses. Reduced alcohol cravings are commonly reported, suggesting dopaminergic effects. On fertility: weight loss can restore fertility in overweight/obese women (leptin is a key fertility signal), producing "Ozempic babies" — unexpected pregnancies. Bakri's clinical approach is a "training wheels" model: lowest effective dose plus lifestyle change, tapering where possible.

Dr. Bakri explicitly says: don't do this at home. Testing a "bunk" overseas Ozempic pen on himself, he injected ~1 mg — roughly 4× the 0.25 mg starting dose — before a hospital night shift, and described projectile vomiting through the whole shift. Huberman's broader caution: micro-dosing and dose-stacking from gray-market sites is widespread and unguided. Sam Altman's reported GLP-1 overdose was attributed to a compounding-pharmacy issue. Titrate slowly, confirm the source and dose, and do it under clinical guidance.

On the horizon: Lilly is reportedly trying to classify retatrutide (39 amino acids) as a biologic by pushing it over 40 amino acids — which would extend patent protection and block compounders. Pricing pressure (some monthly doses now ~$300, down from ~$1,500) and patent strategy are shaping access as much as the science.

13

How to Approach Peptides & What's Coming

Bakri's closing practical advice, and his read on where the field is heading. The short version: the gray market is the wrong place, the foundations come first, and physician-led options are the near-term goal.

  • Where not to look: "research use only" gray-market websites. Contents are unverified — could match a good compounding pharmacy, could be wrong or contaminated.
  • Where to look: under a physician who is educated on peptides, with monitoring and clear endpoints. He expects many more physician-led and telehealth options over the next 6–24 months, which should also push prices down and reveal which compounders are reliable.
  • Foundations first: there is little point layering peptides on top of poor sleep, no morning light, darkness at night and a processed-food diet. Get the basics in place before considering any of this.
  • Monitoring matters: e.g. don't take growth-hormone secretagogues without checking IGF-1; GLP-1 users should be counselled against excessive weight loss (some celebrities clearly lost too much).

The open regulatory question is whether oral peptides (BPC, pinealon) will be treated as supplements — sold next to magnesium and creatine — or as medications. The analogy: oral B12 is a supplement, but injectable B12 needs a prescription. How that distinction lands for peptides is unresolved, and Bakri wants clearer FDA guidance.

To my doctor friends: you now live in a peptide era. You have no choice but to get educated. — Dr. Abud Bakri (paraphrased)
A nomenclature point both speakers push: "peptide" is too blunt a word. They float dividing the field by function — peptides with vs without known receptors, "regenerative" peptides (BPC), "immunogenic"/thymic peptides (thymosin α1, TB-500), and so on — to reduce the confusion the general public is currently navigating in real time.
