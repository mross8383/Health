01

You Are a Team of Rivals

The illusion of a single unified self breaks down on inspection. Eagleman's central frame is that the brain is a parliament of competing neural networks, all with different drives, voting on every decision. Behaviour is the output of whichever coalition wins at a given moment.

The brain contains roughly 86 billion neurons, hooked up into networks that talk to each other at high speed. These networks are not all aligned — they actively compete. The chocolate-chip cookie example: one network wants to eat it (energy), another doesn't (weight), a third negotiates a compromise (eat one, hit the gym). You are not deciding; you are arguing with yourself.

SHIP OF STATEBehaviour at any moment depends on the vote of the "neural parliament" REGRETWhy we look back and disown actions — a different coalition was in charge then KNOW THYSELVESEagleman's update on the Greek admonition — the better you know your sub-selves, the closer you can align with who you want to be
The way your ship of state moves depends on the vote of the neural parliament at any time. — David Eagleman

Different circumstances summon different sub-selves. The "you" at 1am near the cookie jar is not the "you" at 9am planning the day. Treating yourself as one fixed person is a trap because you'll keep being surprised by your own behaviour. This isn't a metaphor — it's structural.

Counterpoint worth noting: the team-of-rivals framing is intuitive and useful, but it is one model, not settled neuroscience. Some researchers argue it overstates how modular the brain is, and that conflict is better described as competing weighted signals within shared circuitry. Useful as a thinking tool; don't confuse the metaphor for the mechanism.
02

The Ulysses Contract

The practical consequence of the team-of-rivals model: if the future you is a different person, the present you can constrain them. A Ulysses contract is any structural commitment made now that prevents bad behaviour later.

DEFINITIONAn action taken now to prevent yourself from behaving badly in the near future CLASSIC CASEAA's first instruction — clear all alcohol out of the house. Don't trust the future you to refuse it. PRINCIPLEConstrain the environment, not the willpower

Sober reflection is unreliable as a guarantee. A festive Saturday or a lonely Sunday will summon a different parliament. The trick is to remove the option before the wrong coalition shows up.

How to apply it to a behaviour change goal

Resolve alone has poor odds — most New Year's resolutions are dead by February. Eagleman's framing for, say, becoming a marathon runner:

  • Identify the distant dream (run a marathon)
  • Work out what actually motivates you in the short term — who you're trying to impress, what you're trying to prove
  • Lock in a Ulysses contract: commit to meeting Bob at 7am every morning to run. The social commitment makes the future you show up.
  • Let the flywheel kick in — body adapts, brain adapts, running gets easier, you do more of it
A new book by Eagleman on the Ulysses contract is scheduled for 2027.
03

Plasticity & the Half-Baked Human Brain

Brain plasticity — coined a century ago by William James — describes the brain's ability to mould to experience and hold the new shape. Unlike other animals, humans drop into the world deliberately under-developed so the environment can finish the job.

HORSE BRAINBorn ready. Does roughly what horses did 100,000 years ago. HUMAN BRAINBorn half-baked. Absorbs everything that came before, then springboards off the top. THE CORTEXThe wrinkly outer 3mm — the bit that is dramatically larger in humans and is a "one-trick pony" of generic plastic tissue THE GAMBLEIf a human doesn't get the right input during critical windows, the under-baked brain doesn't recover

The Romanian orphanage case after Ceaușescu's fall is one of the cleanest illustrations: tens of thousands of children fed but not held or spoken to, on the deliberate policy that attention would make them clingy. The cognitive deficits were severe and enduring. This is the downside of plasticity — the system depends on input, and the absence of input is itself an input.

Critical-window claims are real but often overstated in popular accounts. Eagleman's stronger version — that the very concept of language is unreachable without early exposure — relies heavily on rare cases like Genie, where confounding abuse and malnutrition make the data hard to read. The general direction (earlier is easier, some windows close) is well supported; the categorical "never" is more contested.

The cortex is generic — sensory areas are interchangeable

The textbook colour-coded brain diagram — visual cortex here, auditory cortex there — misleads. The tissue is largely the same stuff and can be reassigned. People born blind have their "visual cortex" recruited for hearing and touch. People born deaf have their "auditory cortex" repurposed. What we call a sensory area is simply whatever input has been wired into it.

This is the foundation for Eagleman's theory of dreaming (see Section 09) and for his broader claim that we can become the sculptors of our own brains by deciding what to feed them.

04

Fluid vs. Crystallised Intelligence

Why your brain becomes harder to change isn't simply decay — it's that you're getting fewer wrong answers about how the world works.

EARLY LIFE
Fluid intelligence
You can learn anything. Drop a baby into 10th-century Mongolia and they become a 10th-century Mongolian.
ADULT LIFE
Crystallised intelligence
You know how to drive, run a business, operate a phone. Internal model is set, so the brain doesn't require as much change.

Eagleman is blunt about the structural cost: your brain peaked at age two. That's when neural connections are densest — an overgrown garden. From there it's pruning. The reason most people interpret this as "plasticity diminishes with age" is that they confuse doesn't change with can't change. The brain changes less because the world keeps confirming its existing model.

When the model is broken, plasticity returns. Eagleman cites the pandemic as an unintended natural experiment — every adult had to reassess basic assumptions about how the world worked (will the bank be open? will there be toilet paper?). Awful, but the kind of forced model-revision adults rarely get.
PRACTICAL FRAMEThe reason brains change less is because they don't have to. To force change, force the brain to be wrong.
05

The Sweet Spot: Frustrating but Achievable

If forcing the brain to be wrong is the mechanism of change, the practical question is how to do that on demand. Eagleman's answer: deliberately seek tasks at the edge of your competence, and abandon things once you're good at them.

  • Stay in the zone where the task is frustrating but achievable — not so easy it confirms what you already know, not so hard it shuts you down
  • Seek novelty. Take on new tasks. Become expert in a field, then drop it.
  • Once you're good at something, dropping it is the highest-yield move for the brain — it forces new roadways instead of strengthening old ones
  • The internet is a feature here, not a bug — exposure to new domains has never been cheaper
Where Brain Change Happens
Once you become good at something, you have to drop that and take on something you're not good at. This is the best thing you can do for your brain. — David Eagleman
06

Cognitive Reserve & the Nuns Study

If the brain's structure is always degenerating, what determines whether degeneration shows up as cognitive decline? Answer: how many alternative pathways you've built.

The Religious Orders Study (the "Nun Study")

Catholic nuns who agreed to donate their brains for autopsy. On post-mortem, a fraction of them showed clear physical signs of Alzheimer's — tissue degenerating with the disease — yet showed no cognitive deficits in life. No memory loss. No decline that anyone noticed.

The explanation: convent life. They lived together until they died, with social conflict, games, chores, responsibilities, and constant interpersonal challenge. Their brains were forced to keep building new roadways even as old tissue died. Eagleman calls this cognitive reserve.

Worth flagging — the Religious Orders Study is real and influential, but the framing here is simplified. Cognitive reserve is genuinely well-evidenced, but the mechanism is debated, and pathology-without-symptoms is a complex finding that has more than one interpretation. The general moral (keep your brain working) is sound; the causal story is less tidy than the anecdote suggests.
CONTRASTPeople who retire at 65, watch TV, social circle shrinks → no challenge, no new pathways, decline accelerates SOCIAL LIFEAmong the most demanding things the brain ever does — you can never predict what another person will say or feel HEARING LOSSA common vicious cycle: hearing declines → parties become unfun → social life shrinks → cognitive load drops
Nothing is as hard for the brain as other people. — Neuroscience saying cited by Eagleman

The framing for what you're doing in your 30s and 40s: you're not just learning for now, you're banking pathways. When degeneration eventually starts, the more roads you've built, the more redundancy you have. Or as Stephen Bartlett summarised it back to him: build pathways now so there's more left over once degeneration begins.

07

What Physically Changes — Visible Brain Adaptations

Plasticity isn't abstract — physical brain structure measurably reorganises in response to what you do.

PIANISTSVisibly larger loop of motor cortex tissue — both hands doing fine work VIOLINISTSThe same enlargement, but only on one side — the bowing hand doesn't get it JUGGLERSMeasurable cortical changes from sustained practice MEDICAL STUDENTSCortical distribution visibly shifts during periods of intense final-exam study

The brain devotes more real estate to whatever it deems relevant. The mechanism for change isn't motivation in any mystical sense — it's repeated, demanding use of a circuit, which the brain reads as a signal to invest more tissue there.

Novice vs. expert brains under load

When two people perform the same task — say juggling — the expert's brain shows less activity, not more. The novice's brain is on fire trying to figure out where to put each hand, what the rules are, what comes next. The expert has burned the routine deep into the circuitry and barely needs to think.

This is the brain's whole goal: practise something enough that it stops costing energy. Useful for survival; less useful if you want continued growth, because mastery is the off-switch for change.

The anterior mid-cingulate cortex — the "willpower muscle"?

Bartlett raises the popular Andrew Huberman framing — that the anterior mid-cingulate cortex is bigger in people who do hard things they don't want to do, and that it functions as a willpower muscle.

Eagleman pushes back gently. He'd reframe it: a larger anterior mid-cingulate is a retrospective indicator of how much hard work someone has done, not necessarily a muscle you can train directly. Brain regions get bigger as a consequence of demand on them — that's true everywhere in the cortex. Calling it the willpower muscle reverses cause and effect.

Worth holding lightly. The "willpower muscle" framing is popular online but not how most neuroscientists characterise the region. Eagleman's correction is the more conservative read.

Exercise is broadly important for brain health. In rats, physical exercise demonstrably increases the trickle of new neurons (neurogenesis); whether this happens to a meaningful degree in humans is still genuinely unsettled. What is settled: sleep, diet and exercise matter for the brain. Not new, but it bears repeating.

08

Virtuous vs. Vicious Friction — Using AI Without Atrophy

If hard things build the brain, doesn't outsourcing thinking to AI cause atrophy? Eagleman's answer is more careful than the popular take. The distinction isn't AI vs. no AI — it's which kind of friction you're cutting.

VICIOUS FRICTION
Cut it ruthlessly
Copying spreadsheet rows. Filling tax forms. Admin. Busy work. There is no honour in doing this manually. Push to AI.
VIRTUOUS FRICTION
Protect it
Thinking through hard problems. Wrestling with structure. Writing your own argument. This is where growth lives — don't let AI do it for you.
CALCULATOR PRECEDENTIn the 1990s there was real debate about letting kids use calculators. We resolved it sensibly — learn long division for a few days, then move on. AI is the same shape of question. SUPER-HUMAN MODEUsed well, AI is "a motorcycle for the mind" (Eagleman's update on Steve Jobs's "bicycle for the mind"). The race now is who uses it best, not whether to use it.

The prompt that builds the brain — "tell me where I'm wrong"

Both Eagleman and Bartlett converge on the same practice. Bring an idea to your AI and ask it to argue against you. Eagleman's habit: pitch a half-formed idea, then say "give me pros and cons, tell me why this is wrong." Engage with the counter-arguments seriously. This is virtuous friction.

The instinct to avoid being wrong is strong and social — admitting it to a human is embarrassing. AI strips out the social cost, which makes it easier to actually update. AIs do default to flattery, but you can instruct them to be brutally honest, and once you do, the feedback loop is genuinely useful.

Eagleman's view on what to teach the next generation: there are really only two things worth teaching — critical thinking and creativity. Specific technical skills (his example: programming) may already be obsolete as taught.

The "effort phenomenon" — why AI slop is so irritating

Psychology studies consistently show people value things that look like they took effort. Bottle-cap sculptures sell for more than blank canvases. Real diamonds command a premium over chemically identical lab-grown ones — because mother nature took hundreds of millions of years on the first one.

This is why obvious AI emails and LinkedIn posts read as offensive. They signal "I didn't care enough to think." The damage isn't only to the recipient — the sender almost certainly didn't learn anything from generating it either. Whatever cognitive growth might have come from doing the hard thing was traded for a quick output.

Practical heuristic: if the same friction would otherwise be done by an intern doing busywork, send it to AI. If the friction is the part where your own model of the world gets stretched, do it yourself — possibly with AI as a sparring partner, but not as a substitute.

Where AI is creative — and where it isn't

Eagleman defines creativity as bending, breaking and blending what you've absorbed into new remixes. By that definition AI is massively creative — it's exactly what it does. What it can't do is selection. It can generate a hundred images, but it can't know which one will land for you. It can write jokes structurally but can't reliably break a pattern in a way that's funny — it doesn't think of the punchline first and then build toward it.

Bartlett pushes back with examples — Gemini predicting YouTube thumbnail winners with unsettling accuracy, predicting drop-off points in long videos. Eagleman concedes the point but flags the catch: the AI may be picking up on dimensions humans can't see (font size, colour ratios), and it can only ever optimise toward the centre of the existing distribution. The genuinely new song or image — the one that breaks out — sits at the edges, where AI can generate it but not pick it out.

09

Why We Dream — Defending the Visual Cortex

Eagleman proposes a relatively new theory of dreaming that follows directly from his work on plasticity. The thesis: dreams are not for processing memories or rehearsing fears or revealing the unconscious. They are a defensive mechanism to stop other senses from colonising the visual cortex during the half of every day we spend in darkness.

THE PROBLEMEarth rotates into darkness for ~half of every 24 hours. The visual cortex is starved of input. THE THREATCortex is generic — unused territory gets repurposed by other senses. Lose-it-or-lose-it tissue. THE DEFENCEEvery ~90 minutes, an ancient circuit in the midbrain blasts random activity into the visual cortex. The brain weaves it into a story. That's a dream.

The Harvard blindfold experiment

Normally sighted people were blindfolded tightly. Within 60 minutes, the visual cortex began responding to sound and touch — takeover was already starting. That speed was what tipped Eagleman toward the defence theory: if the visual area can be invaded that fast, the brain needs an active mechanism to hold the territory through the long darkness of sleep.

The cross-species evidence

Eagleman's lab compared 25 species of primates. Two measurements: how plastic each species' brain is, and how much REM (rapid eye movement) sleep they get. The correlation was, in his telling, perfect — the more plastic the brain, the more dreaming.

  • Humans, the most plastic, dream the most
  • Infants spend roughly 50% of sleep time in REM. Plasticity is highest then; the visual cortex needs the most defending.
  • As you age, REM share declines — the territory is more entrenched and needs less protection
  • Species that drop into the world fully baked have minimal REM
  • Even blind mole rats — which lost vision over evolutionary time — still dream, because the underlying defence circuitry is so ancient
Read the strength of this carefully. A correlation across 25 species, even a tight one, is suggestive but not proof of mechanism. Other theories of dreaming (memory consolidation, emotional regulation, threat simulation) are not mutually exclusive with Eagleman's, and the field is far from consensus. His framing is interesting and elegant; treat it as a leading hypothesis, not settled science.
Evolution gave us this TV that comes on at nighttime when the real TV — our real life — turns off, just to keep that part of the brain doing something so it doesn't atrophy. — Stephen Bartlett, summarising the theory

A consequence Eagleman is willing to follow through: most dreams are bizarre and meaningless. If it's random activity stitched into a story, occasional dreams might feel resonant the way a randomly opened book passage might — but the brain is the one supplying meaning, not the dream. He suspects the deeper symbolic interpretations of dreams have always been over-reading noise.

The Hidden Spectrum of Inner Experience

A theme Eagleman returns to: the assumption that other people's inner worlds resemble your own is almost always wrong. The variation across the population is much wider than people realise, and visualisation is one of the cleanest examples.

THE TESTImagine an ant on a purple-and-white tablecloth crawling toward a jar of red jelly. What do you actually see? HYPERFANTASIAA vivid mental image — colours, motion, almost like watching a film APHANTASIANothing visual at all. No image. The thought is conceptual, motoric, auditory or olfactory — but not pictorial. DISTRIBUTIONSpread evenly across the population

Eagleman is aphantasic — he doesn't see his children in his head as images. He has a rich notion of being with them through motor, auditory and olfactory imagery, but no visual picture. He notes the surprising counterintuitive finding from Pixar founder Ed Catmull (also aphantasic): when Catmull surveyed Pixar's staff, many of the best animators and directors turned out to be aphantasic too. The likely reason: an aphantasic child who wants to draw has to look hard at the actual subject and stay in dialogue with the page, while a hyperfantasic child draws what they already think a horse looks like.

Crucially: aphantasia translates to no measurable cognitive deficit. People accomplish tasks via dozens of different routes — visual, motoric, conceptual, auditory. Different inner experience, same external capability.

Synaesthesia — blended senses

At least 3% of the population has synaesthesia — the senses bleed into each other. Letters trigger colours (J is green, M is blue). Music triggers visual experience. Tastes produce textures on the fingertips. The specifics are different for every synaesthete. It is not a disease or disorder — Eagleman is firm on this — it is an alternative perceptual reality.

The broader point: across dozens of axes Eagleman has studied, almost no two people are having the same inner experience. The folk model of "we all see the world basically the same way" is wrong.

Brains vs. AI — Why the Resemblance Is Superficial

Artificial neural networks were inspired by the brain, but the inspiration is superficial. Eagleman is sharper on this than the popular framing suggests.

THE ANALOGYImagine units connected to other units, each connection with a weight. Strip out everything else. WHAT WAS STRIPPEDA single neuron is as complex as a city — it carries the entire human genome and traffics millions of proteins. None of that exists in an artificial network. JAGGED INTELLIGENCEWhy AI does something brilliant in one moment and something incoherent the next — convergent on intelligence, but via a fundamentally different structure

Two structural differences worth holding onto:

  • One-trial learning — humans encounter a pomegranate once and have it for life. AI needs thousands or millions of examples for anything, and a training phase costing billions of dollars before it can answer a single question.
  • No team of rivals — AI doesn't have competing drives, appetites, emotions, or sub-selves. You don't want ChatGPT to be hungry or anxious. Useful, but a profoundly different kind of system.
  • Outside-only access — AI watches human behaviour from outside. It can model what people do; it cannot know what it is to be a human from the inside. Whether that matters in practice is a genuinely open question.

The renaissance of the human

Eagleman's prediction: a renaissance in live performance, live theatre, in-person talks. He has personally seen demand for in-person speaking go up since the AI boom, not down — people specifically don't want a virtual avatar of him giving the talk. Earlier waves of technology were predicted to kill concerts (Napster) but only made the live experience more valued.

The corollary for healthcare and other domains: AI takes over admin and paperwork; humans focus on what only humans can do — holding a hand, being present, meeting in person. The technology may finally be delivering on a promise it has long made and never quite kept: making us more human, not less.

Out-Groups & the Complexification of Relationships

When Eagleman is asked what people most need to hear that hasn't yet been covered, this is what he goes to. The brain has dedicated social circuitry for treating others as fully human — and it gets dialled down when we cluster into in-groups and out-groups.

When we encounter a homeless person, a drug addict, or a political opponent and pre-classify them as "out-group", the social circuitry that perceives them as a person quietens. They become an object to navigate around. Eagleman's prescription is what he calls complexification: deliberately surfacing the cross-cutting facts about a person — they grew up in your hometown, love the same dogs, surf, share an interest. The more cross-cuts you find, the harder it is to keep them in a flat out-group category.

EYE CONTACT & CONVERSATIONAmong the simplest practices that keep social circuitry online UNDERSTANDING ≠ AGREEINGTrying to model someone's internal worldview is not endorsement — it's keeping the perception-of-personhood circuit working AI'S POSSIBLE ROLECould be used to challenge dismissals — "what if I told you this story about a person from that group?"

On echo chambers — Eagleman's contrarian read

Eagleman is more optimistic about online discourse than the prevailing narrative. His argument: echo chambers are nothing new — your village 500 years ago was already an echo chamber, and so was state-controlled media in the USSR. At least now you are aware that other points of view exist, even if those views drive you crazy.

Faced with a choice between state-controlled single-narrative media and the messy, polarised, algorithmically-curated open spectrum, he'd take the spectrum.

Bartlett's pushback is worth holding alongside this: the design of modern algorithmic feeds exploits dopamine the way slot machines do, and the rise of interest-graph distribution (the algorithm decides who sees what, not your follower list) creates a different kind of echo chamber than the village ever did. Eagleman's response is essentially that humans don't actually only want slot machines — we go to Vegas occasionally, not constantly — and there's a market opening for a social network designed around connection rather than rage. Both views have merit.
13

Staving Off Dementia — The Concrete Advice

When asked directly what he would do to fend off dementia, Eagleman's answer is blunt and short.

RULE 1
Keep the brain active until the day you die
No coasting. The structure is always degenerating; the only counter is to keep building new pathways.
RULE 2
Drop things as soon as you're good at them
If sudoku is now easy, stop doing sudoku. Pick up something you're bad at. The brain only changes when forced to.

The mechanism: brain structure is always degrading. With Alzheimer's, much faster. The defence is to be constantly building new roadways and fashioning new paths that haven't been walked before — so that even as parts of the network fall apart, alternative routes remain from A to B.

SLEEP, DIET, EXERCISEAll matter for the brain. Not new advice. Worth taking seriously anyway. SOCIAL LIFEFrom Section 06 — possibly the single highest-leverage cognitive demand. Don't let it shrink. ALCOHOLEagleman notes the cultural shift away from drinking, especially in younger generations. Worth flagging that the trade-off isn't one-way — his anecdote about a younger friend wanting drinking back because parties have become socially awkward.
The simplest summary of the whole guide: the brain changes less because it doesn't have to. To keep changing, deliberately make it have to. Find the frustrating-but-achievable edge, sit there, and move on as soon as the edge becomes flat.
