Philosophy & Approach

Dr. Mills's approach is not to treat disease — it is to improve the performance of the body's underlying systems so that disease cannot take hold. He rarely focuses on the diagnosis. He looks for where the body's performance broke down, how far back that goes, and what was upstream.

Core Principles

  • "I can't do anything about the sea and the waves, but I can help you build a better boat." — the goal is resilience, not just symptom removal
  • Plants were humanity's primary source of medicine for almost all of history. We have evolved alongside them.
  • Most of his herbs have an immediate effect — he asks new patients to call him the next day, not wait weeks
  • The patient is the boss — Dr. Mills gives guidance, the patient discovers what works for them
  • He dislikes the word "anti-inflammatory" — he prefers "modulating" or "supporting" inflammation, because inflammation is a defence, not the enemy
  • Only 5 countries in the world are not broadly using herbal medicine: UK, US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand — all originally English-speaking white settler countries. The rest of the world uses plants as routine medicine.

What He Treats — and How He Thinks About It

Most patients who come to him have been around the block — they've had their conditions for years, tried conventional treatment, and been told "nothing more can be done." His approach: go backwards and fix what wasn't fixed earlier.

  • ~25% of patients are living with cancer — he supports quality of life and function, not treating the cancer itself
  • ~30–35% have chronic inflammatory conditions — Crohn's, ulcerative colitis, psoriasis, autoimmune diseases
  • He starts with digestion, liver function, kidney function, and circulation — wherever the original disruption appears to have started
  • He always asks: "What was going on in this person's life before the condition appeared? What was the original insult?"

Inflammation — Friend, Not Enemy

One of Dr. Mills's strongest positions: inflammation is not the disease. It is the body's defence mechanism doing its job. The question is always: why is the inflammation necessary, and what can we do upstream to reduce the need for it?

Acute vs. Chronic Inflammation — Two Very Different Things

Ibuprofen & NSAIDs — Dr. Mills's View

Ibuprofen is derived from salicylic acid — a plant substance that gave us aspirin. NSAIDs work by cutting the inflammatory process entirely. That may be necessary short-term, but it comes with a cost.

  • Short-term acute inflammation: overwhelmingly the advice is not to suppress it — the body is doing its job
  • Chronic inflammation: always ask why the inflammation is there. What is being dumped on the joint or tissue? Go upstream.
  • For arthritis specifically: the joint has poor circulation by design (two surfaces pressing together). Metabolic waste deposits there — like a U-bend under a sink. The inflammation is trying to flush it clean.
  • Using ibuprofen stops the cleanup process. The better approach: help the body do the cleanup more efficiently by improving circulation and removing the upstream source of metabolic waste.
Turmeric/curcumin was found in a 2016 meta-analysis of RCTs to be comparable to ibuprofen for pain relief — but works via a different mechanism that supports rather than suppresses inflammation modulation.

The Gut & Microbiome

Dr. Mills starts almost every treatment with the gut — not because it's fashionable, but because it's where plants go, where most of the immune system lives, and where most chronic problems originate. "We are walking gut bags — there are more bacteria in the gut than cells in our body."

The Microbiome — What It Actually Does

  • More bacteria in the gut than human cells — they have ~100× our genetic capacity
  • The microbiome runs the show — the heart, kidney, and brain only function as they do because they work with the microbiome
  • The microbiome is required to process polyphenols — without a healthy microbiome, you cannot unlock the benefits of coloured foods
  • Curcumin from turmeric works primarily via the microbiome — it barely enters the blood (~1–2%), but the microbiome converts it into active anti-inflammatory compounds
  • Antibiotics reduce microbiome biodiversity — this loss, even if partial, affects nearly every system in the body
  • Coloured fruits and vegetables (polyphenols) are prebiotic — they directly feed the beneficial species
Aim for 30 different plant varieties per week. Diversity of plant types creates diversity of bacterial species. No one knows exactly which species you personally need — so give as many as possible.

Probiotics, Prebiotics & Postbiotics

ProbioticsLiving organisms — yogurt, kimchi, kefir. Must survive stomach acid to be effective. Some strains do make it through. PrebioticsFoods that feed the good bacteria — coloured plants, root vegetables, garlic, inulin. The most powerful intervention. PostbioticsProducts the bacteria make — short-chain fatty acids, vitamin K2, and the activated forms of polyphenols. You get these from fermented foods and from having a healthy microbiome processing plant foods.
A damaged microbiome (from antibiotics, poor diet, or stress) means even good supplements are less effective — the microbiome can't convert them. Fix the gut first.

Warming Plants — Colds, Pain & Circulation

Old medicine divided remedies into warming (stimulating circulation) and cooling (stimulating digestion). Warming remedies are for conditions that feel better with heat — colds, joint pain that wants a hot water bottle, menstrual cramps, headaches that want a hot pack.

Use Warming Remedies When...

  • You feel cold, chilly, want to wrap up
  • Pain is better with a hot water bottle or heat pack
  • Headache prefers heat (70% of migraine sufferers)
  • Menstrual cramps, joint pain that responds to warmth
  • Cold / flu onset — feeling "cold" is diagnostic

Use Cooling Remedies When...

  • You feel hot and want to cool down
  • Pain is better with an ice pack (30% of migraine sufferers)
  • Fever — the body is overheating
  • Digestive stimulation needed (bitters)
  • Poor appetite after illness

Ginger & Cinnamon — The Foundation Remedy

The combination Dr. Mills calls "made in heaven." Both are warming — ginger stimulates pain fibres which open blood vessels (hyperaemia), loosening mucus in airways and warming the body throughout.

How to makeGrate a thumb-sized piece of fresh ginger into a mug. Add 1 tsp of Ceylon cinnamon (tightly rolled, many fine layers — not the single large curled Cassia bark). Add hot water. Sieve and drink. Use forColds, flu, headaches that want heat, menstrual cramps, joint pain that responds to warmth, chills Also worksTurmeric (same family as ginger), chillies, black pepper — all warming Not for everyoneSome people find ginger too stimulating — it heats them up too much or upsets the stomach. Listen to your body.

Ginger stimulates pain fibres throughout mucous membranes — opening blood vessels, loosening mucus, helping flush the airways. If you have a lung issue, you will start to bring up more material after drinking it. The warmth you feel is real — not imagined.

Cayenne / Capsicum — For Joint Pain

Applied externally, not eaten. Capsicum (chilli) plasters/creams directly bring blood into the joint — meaning the inflammation doesn't have to do it, and therefore the pain reduces. This was the standard Native American and European (mustard) approach to joint pain for centuries.

  • Capsicum plasters are available in pharmacies as standard prescriptions for pain
  • For arthritic fingers: a warm mustard bath (dilute yellow mustard in warm water, soak fingers) can ease pain quickly
  • The heat you feel is stimulating pain fibres — there is no actual tissue burning; chilli does not damage mucosa

Other Warming Herbs

  • Cardamom — Dr. Mills's personal favourite. Warming, sustaining, nourishing for digestion. Used in India and the Middle East. Used in China as a convalescent tonic when digestion is weak after illness. "One of my favourite remedies for when people are run low."
  • Fennel tea — warming digestive remedy
  • Turmeric — warming, anti-inflammatory modulator. Works via the microbiome. Pairs well with black pepper (raises curcumin availability slightly). Best consumed as food — in curries, with a variety of spices.

Cooling Plants & Bitters

Cooling remedies stimulate digestion and were historically used for fever. Bitters are the most important category — they trigger taste buds which hardwire to digestive hormones, increasing all digestive activity.

Bitters — How They Work

Bitter taste buds in the mouth are hardwired to the stomach via hormonal pathways. When triggered, they switch on all digestive activity — increasing gastric juices, bile flow, appetite, and blood supply to the gut. This is why a bitter aperitif before a meal was a universal tradition.

  • Wormwood — the most valued European bitter. The French name is vermouth. Used as an aperitif before meals to stimulate appetite.
  • Dandelion — a familiar bitter, now available as a soft drink (particularly in the US)
  • Burdock — another classic bitter, now also a soft drink
  • Coffee — an accessible bitter. Used originally as a post-meal digestive in the Middle East (ground, not filtered). An espresso without sugar is a bitter. Coffee is beneficial for its soluble fibre and polyphenols — NOT primarily for the caffeine.
  • Artichoke leaf — a liver and digestive bitter. Used widely in France for liver and fat-processing problems.
Bitters are used when appetite is poor, especially after illness. They can kick-start the digestive system when it has stalled. Effect is noticeable within an hour.

Peppermint — The Classic Cooling Herb

The most widely known cooling remedy. If someone prefers peppermint tea to ginger tea, that tells Dr. Mills something useful about what their system needs — they are constitutionally cooling rather than warming.

Use forFever management, digestive discomfort, headaches that prefer ice packs, general cooling Simple diagnosticWould you prefer ginger tea or peppermint tea right now? The answer narrows down which type of remedy your body is calling for.

Antibiotics — The Real Crisis

Dr. Mills describes antibiotic resistance as one of the most urgent health threats of our time — and he is not alone. The WHO declared it a top global health threat in 2023. It is already responsible for over 1.27 million deaths annually (2019 figure, rising).

Why the Crisis Is Real

  • Every time you take an antibiotic, you select for resistant bacteria — natural selection in action. 1,000 bacteria, kill 999 — the one survivor doubles every 20 minutes. It is now resistant.
  • 236 million antibiotic prescriptions given in the US in 2022 (roughly 7 per 10 people). At least 30% are unnecessary.
  • 30 million antibiotic prescriptions in the UK in 2020
  • Antibiotics are useless against viruses — they do nothing for colds, flu, or COVID. Yet they are routinely prescribed for viral symptoms by harassed doctors responding to patient demand.
  • Pipeline of new antibiotics is poor — little financial incentive to develop them
  • Hospital-acquired infections are increasingly untreatable — going to hospital for surgery is becoming a real risk of encountering an antibiotic-resistant infection
Dr. Mills's position: take antibiotics when you genuinely need them — serious bacterial infections. Do not take them for colds, flu, or viral respiratory problems. They do not help and actively contribute to the global crisis.

What to Do Instead for Colds, Flu & Upper Respiratory Infections

  • Ginger and cinnamon tea — warming, opens airways, stimulates mucus clearance
  • Echinacea liquid extract — activates frontline immune cells in throat and sinuses
  • Echinacea + frankincense/boswellia + liquorice — combined for mouth, throat, and sinus infections
  • Garlic intensive (see below) — for gut or lung infections
  • Vitamin D, Vitamin C, Zinc — evidence for improving resilience in the face of infection (not replacements for antibiotics in serious infection)

Brain Health & Dementia Prevention

The emerging area Dr. Mills is most excited about. Research is increasingly focusing on the blood-brain barrier (now called the neurovascular unit — NVU) rather than amyloid plaques. Most things that protect the NVU are plants.

The Neurovascular Unit (Blood-Brain Barrier)

What was called the blood-brain barrier is now understood as a dynamic, living interface — the neurovascular unit. It controls what enters and exits the brain. Protecting it reduces dementia risk.

  • Green tea — polyphenols modulate the NVU. Populations that drink green tea regularly have lower rates of cognitive decline and Alzheimer's. Drink it — supplement form less effective.
  • Rosemary — volatile oils inhaled directly enter the brain via the olfactory lobe. Clinical trial showed improved cognitive performance. Shakespeare's Hamlet: "Rosemary, that's for remembrance" — everyone knew this.
  • Dark chocolate/cacao — brain blood flow improved within minutes of consumption in studies. Polyphenols reach the NVU.
  • Ginkgo biloba — prescription medicine in Europe for cardiovascular and cognitive problems. Works on NVU similar to green tea.
  • Turmeric/curcumin — early preclinical evidence for brain health. Note: preclinical = test tubes and animals, not yet proven in large human trials.
  • Matcha — likely more beneficial than standard green tea (higher quality, more concentrated polyphenols). Evidence is building.

Women's Health, PCOS & Fertility

Dr. Mills has approximately 13 "herb babies" — pregnancies in women who had been struggling to conceive, who became pregnant after herbal treatment stabilised their menstrual cycles. Many women's health problems begin elsewhere — in the liver, digestion, or blood sugar metabolism.

PCOS, Insulin Resistance & the Menstrual Cycle

  • 80% of women with PCOS have some degree of insulin resistance — even those who are not overweight
  • Insulin resistance disrupts the hormonal choreography of the menstrual cycle — causing irregular periods, cysts on ovaries, and reduced fertility
  • Reducing sugar and refined carbohydrates improves insulin sensitivity → menstrual cycles regularise → fertility improves
  • Keto diet has been observed to abolish or significantly reduce menstrual irregularity in women with insulin resistance

Acid Reflux & PPIs (Omeprazole)

Omeprazole (a PPI — proton pump inhibitor) is the most widely prescribed drug in both the UK and US. Dr. Mills regards it as mostly unnecessary, difficult to stop, and associated with growing long-term risks.

The Problem with PPIs

  • Acid reflux is NOT caused by too much acid — it is caused by acid going to the wrong place (refluxing back into the oesophagus)
  • Stomach acid is essential — it sterilises food, breaks down proteins, and makes food immunologically safe for the body
  • PPIs shut down acid production — solving the symptom while eliminating a critical defence
  • Long-term PPI use: growing associations with cancers and dementias
  • Rebound effect: stopping PPIs causes far worse reflux — the body has upregulated acid production. This makes people feel they must keep taking it.
  • GERD is literally diagnosed by the fact that omeprazole makes it better — it is defined by its treatment
Do not stop PPIs abruptly — the rebound effect is real and severe. Weaning off requires a slow taper using the raft principle to manage symptoms during the transition.

The Raft Principle — Natural Alternative

Instead of blocking acid, put a physical mucous barrier on top of the stomach contents so the acid cannot reflux. Take it after the last mouthful of a meal.

GavisconSeaweed gum with minerals — forms a raft. Widely available. Breaks down within 2 hours — not a drug, just a physical barrier. Slippery ElmNorth American plant powder — forms a mucous paste in the stomach. Take as a tablet (not powder). Provides the same raft effect. Aloe VeraMucilaginous — coats and soothes the stomach lining. TimingAfter the last mouthful of a meal — before food can push acid back up
The raft is a carbohydrate — it digests within a couple of hours. No drug, no side effects, no dependency. Can be used during PPI withdrawal to manage symptoms while the stomach rebalances.

Polyphenols, Colour & Eating the Rainbow

Every colour in a plant is produced by a specific class of polyphenol. Each has different effects. 95% of polyphenols require the microbiome to activate them — they are not absorbed directly. A damaged microbiome = diminished polyphenol benefit.

Colour Guide

Purple / BlueAnthocyanins — the most powerful. Eyesight, brain health, blood pressure control, circulatory health. Sources: beetroot, berries, red/black grapes, purple carrot, purple broccoli. Dr. Mills: "We are probably purple deficient." RedLycopene and other carotenoids — cardiovascular and cancer prevention. Sources: tomatoes, red peppers, red onions. GreenChlorophyll, lutein, glucosinolates — gut health, hormonal metabolism, liver. Sources: broccoli (the cabbage family), dark leafy greens, herbs. Orange / YellowCarotenoids, quercetin — immune support, cardiovascular. Sources: carrots, turmeric, squash, onions. White / TanAllicin (garlic), inulin (chicory, onion), fibre. Prebiotic effects.
Start with roots (carrots, beetroot, parsnips — powerful prebiotic fibre), then greens (broccoli, cabbage family), then grains and legumes (lentils, beans, peas). Aim for as much variety as possible.

Organically Grown Plants — Why They're More Potent

A plant that grows organically — without pesticides — has to fight its own battles. Polyphenols are part of the plant's defence mechanism. An organic plant produces more polyphenols because it needs them. A pesticide-protected plant doesn't need to make as many.

  • Wild or foraged plants have the highest polyphenol content of all
  • Organic is better than conventionally grown, which is still better than nothing
  • Industrial monoculture plants (rows sprayed with herbicide) are the lowest polyphenol option

Fasting — Dr. Mills's View

Dr. Mills supports intermittent fasting in principle — it reflects how humans evolved. Hunter-gatherers ate intermittently by necessity; the feast-fast rhythm is baked into our physiology. However, he cautions that extended fasting is not right for everyone.

  • Intermittent fasting is part of our evolutionary design — we were built to eat intermittently, not continuously
  • The fundamental principle is sound — but extended fasts (7-day water fasts etc.) require checking that your metabolism, digestion, and hormones are in a good enough state first
  • If underlying functions are not at prime, extended fasting can be damaging — get advice before attempting anything beyond 24 hours
  • The body was designed to feast and fast — the problem is modern life has removed the fasting half entirely
