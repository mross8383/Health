The Perimenopause Timeline

The biggest misconception is that perimenopause arrives in the 50s. Shepherd argues it typically begins in the late 30s and runs through the 40s — a decade-long transition that most women don't recognise until symptoms become undeniable.

A menstrual cycle is not proof that nothing is shifting. Estrogen and progesterone can still fluctuate enough to produce a cycle while already failing to deliver adequate signalling to the brain, muscle, skin, gut, bone and fat-distribution systems that also depend on them. Nothing in the body changes overnight; hormone change is a slow, years-long process.

LATE 30sEarliest perimenopausal symptoms can appear. Flux between estrogen and progesterone begins. 40sSymptoms typically intensify — more frequent, more severe. Cycle may still be regular, irregular, or infrequent. 45–55Late perimenopause into early menopause — the ideal window for starting HRT. MENOPAUSEDefined by 12 months without a cycle. Estrogen has "flatlined" — ovaries no longer release enough to produce a cycle. POST-MENO~40% of a woman's life is spent post-menopausal.
If there was just one way to torture a woman, it would be the estrogen lever — saggy skin, mood disorders, libido leaving the building, poor sleep, brain fog, crushing fatigue, all with one little lever. — Gary Brecka (paraphrased)
Key reframe: symptoms are not dismissible if labs look "normal." Labs show hormone levels at a snapshot in time; they don't show how the body's estrogen receptors are experiencing that level. The experiential picture matters as much as the number.
02

Early Symptoms to Watch For

Most women hit "the wall" only once hot flashes arrive — but the trail of symptoms starts years earlier. Shepherd's own first symptom was brain fog, and it took her roughly six months to connect it despite being a menopause specialist.

  • Brain fog — loss of executive function, clarity, memory
  • Sleep changes — waking early, struggling to get going, no longer a "morning person"
  • Mood shifts — anxiety, irritability, frustration, tearfulness
  • Libido decline
  • Skin changes — loss of elasticity, jawline changes, texture
  • Night sweats and hot flashes (often the "obvious" late signal)
  • Frozen shoulder / adhesive capsulitis — under-recognised link to estrogen loss
  • Fatigue that doesn't match sleep tracking data
  • Weight gain and water retention
  • Period changes — infrequent, irregular, or heavier

The key is consistency. One bad night of sleep or one forgetful week is not a signal. The signal is when something recurs, even if not frequently, and doesn't resolve. That pattern is worth discussing with a clinician who takes the experiential picture seriously.

Brecka's wife had frozen shoulder so severe that bending to unbuckle a ski boot brought her to the ground. Three weeks on hormone therapy resolved libido, deepened sleep, and the frozen shoulder cleared about 10 days later. An orthopedic surgeon had wanted to do manipulation under anesthesia to break the adhesions.
"The plumber always has a leaky faucet." Shepherd notes that even experts miss it in themselves — denial and attributing symptoms to stress are near-universal. Self-awareness is the entry point.
03

Testing: What to Measure and Why

Testing in perimenopause is tricky because hormones are fluctuating by design. A single blood draw shows a moment, not a trajectory. Shepherd treats labs as a starting point for conversation — not a gate to treatment.

The conventional physician training is to hunt for abnormalities. That approach misses perimenopause, where values can sit "within normal limits" while the patient is experiencing a clear hormonal transition. The receptor-level experience is what drives symptoms, not the serum number.

Shepherd's suggested panel

ESTROGENBaseline reference; interpret against symptom picture. PROGESTERONECounterpart to estrogen; flux signals perimenopause. FSHFollicle-stimulating hormone — helps situate where you are in the transition. CORTISOLChronic stress is a driver of symptom severity. THYROIDOverlaps heavily with perimenopause symptoms; must be ruled in or out. VITAMIN DCommonly deficient; needed for bone, mood, immune function. HBA1C3-month glucose picture — see Section 07.

Dutch test vs blood work

Shepherd sees the Dutch test (a dried urine panel) as a useful follow-through rather than a starting point. It shows estradiol and progesterone metabolites — how hormones are being processed and where peaks and troughs sit. Better suited to someone already investigating than to a first screen.

Brecka reports his wife's Dutch test mapped almost perfectly onto her subjective symptoms — nearly every complaint had a corresponding finding.

A "normal" blood panel does not mean there is no hormonal transition happening. Normal ranges are population-level, not receptor-level. If labs look fine but the experience is compromised, the conversation about treatment is still valid.
04

Hormone Therapy & the WHI Fallout

The Women's Health Initiative (WHI) study and its media coverage arguably caused a generation of women to avoid HRT. The FDA removal of the black-box warning on estrogen therapy reset the conversation, but the reversal has not yet reached most patients or most prescribers.

What the WHI got wrong

The WHI's original aim was cardiovascular — could hormones reduce heart disease risk, the leading cause of death in women. The design problem was the population. Participants were on average older than menopausal age (late 50s into 60s), included smokers, and included women with pre-existing cardiovascular disease. This is not the population that benefits most from HRT.

The resulting alarmist messaging caused two cascading failures: prescribers stopped offering hormones on "do no harm" grounds, and patients came to believe they would harm themselves by taking them. The benefits — symptom relief plus long-term preventive effects on brain, bone, heart and glucose — were buried.

The "good, better, best" window

BESTLate perimenopause into early menopause, roughly ages 45–55, within the first 5–10 years of no cycle. BETTERStarting later than ideal but before significant inflammatory damage to heart, vessels and brain has accumulated. LATERStill potentially beneficial, but the preventive window for cardiovascular and neurological protection narrows.

Starting later does not mean no benefit — but the preventive case weakens as vascular changes (atherosclerosis, etc.) progress.

Why estrogen matters beyond symptoms

Estrogen receptors are distributed across multiple organ systems. When estrogen drops, many tissues lose a key signalling input at once. Shepherd's short list of what estrogen supports:

  • Glucose metabolism — loss of estrogen worsens insulin response
  • Inflammation — estrogen is a potent anti-inflammatory
  • Lean muscle mass — loss compounds age-related sarcopenia
  • Gut health — gut wall integrity and permeability
  • Bone density
  • Heart muscle and vasculature
  • Brain — cognition, memory, executive function
  • Skin elasticity and connective tissue
50 million women have unnecessarily suffered for decades because of one misread study. — episode framing, per Brecka
Even after the black-box removal, many prescribers have not updated their approach. Shepherd's read: both patients and doctors still operate on the old caution. A patient may need to seek a menopause-literate clinician rather than assume their GP is current.

Autoimmune Disease & Why It Targets Women

Roughly 82% of autoimmune diagnoses occur in women. Through the 40s and 50s there is a parabolic spike where men and women — who tracked together earlier — diverge sharply. Shepherd's framing is multi-factorial: biology, chronic stress, trauma, and the drop in estrogen's anti-inflammatory effect.

HORMONAL
Estrogen is anti-inflammatory
When it drops in midlife, inflammatory conditions including autoimmune disease are unmasked.
STRESS
Chronic sympathetic state
Prolonged cortisol load degrades immune regulation. Autoimmunity "preys on the weak."
TRAUMA
The body keeps score
Shepherd references Gabor Maté — unprocessed trauma can surface externally as autoimmune disease.
SOCIAL
Caregiver burden
Women are socialised to put themselves last. Self-neglect compounds into physiological stress.

Autoimmune disease is, mechanically, the body's cells attacking themselves. Shepherd emphasises mind-body regulation not as alternative medicine but as direct intervention: what happens in the mind directs what the body's cells do. This is why meditation has measurable effects on disease progression.

Brecka's anecdotal audience-poll observation: in large audiences worldwide, around 75–80% of hands go up when he asks who has (or loves someone with) an autoimmune diagnosis. At least half of those hands go up again when he asks about multiple autoimmune diagnoses.
Women are taught not to feel safe in their own bodies. They are emotional absorbers of everyone else around them, and that wreaks havoc internally. — Dr. Jessica Shepherd (paraphrased)
06

Lifestyle Foundations — Gut, Nutrition, Protein

Hormones are layered on top of lifestyle, not a replacement for it. Shepherd's framework prioritises gut, nutrition and protein as the base — without them, HRT's benefits are blunted.

Gut health in midlife

The gut has estrogen receptors. Both aging and estrogen decline increase intestinal permeability — more of what should stay in leaks out, and absorption degrades. This drives vitamin deficiencies and inflammation, and the gut-brain axis connects both to cognitive symptoms.

  • Minimise processed foods — still a major share of the Western diet
  • Whole foods, "make your plate the rainbow"
  • Anti-inflammatory foods as the organising principle
  • Less cooked / less processed options where possible

Protein — the under-eaten macro

Protein intake in women is, in Shepherd's view, woefully inadequate. Protein supports the lean muscle mass that is the body's metabolic currency, and the gut-brain axis means it also supports cognitive clarity. Starting the day with protein rather than a high-glycemic breakfast keeps cortisol and glucose in check through the morning.

Common deficiencies Shepherd sees

VITAMIN DMost common deficiency in her patient population. VITAMIN B12Frequently low, especially with gut permeability issues. FIBRENeeds to be raised in most Western diets. PROTEINUnder-consumed relative to muscle-preservation needs.

Whole foods first. Supplementation fills gaps that diet alone doesn't close, but it is not a substitute.

Environmental burden compounds the picture: hormone-mimicking chemicals in cosmetics, microplastics, mold and mycotoxins, heavy metals, glyphosate, paraquat. Any one may not be catastrophic alone; the total load is what overwhelms the system.
07

Blood Sugar — HbA1c and CGM

Loss of estrogen directly worsens glucose metabolism. For midlife women, blood sugar management moves from a diabetic's concern to a universal one. Brecka's framing: "when glucose goes out of control, the dominoes go down fast."

FASTINGA single snapshot. Often "fine" and therefore dismissive. HBA1CGlycosylated hemoglobin — the 3-month picture of how the body manages glucose. Better proxy for trouble brewing. CGMContinuous glucose monitor — the individualised real-time picture. Reveals how specific foods and behaviours affect you.

Why a CGM matters: generic nutrition templates break down at the individual level. Shepherd notes that ethnic background changes how some carbohydrates (e.g. rice) are metabolised. Without personal data, people follow templates that may not suit them.

Behaviours Shepherd and Brecka saw move glucose in real time: a 10–15 minute walk after a meal, a cold plunge (drops glucose significantly via the stress response), sauna, high-fat / high-protein / high-fibre meals instead of high-glycemic ones, starting the day with protein instead of sugar.
The morning glucose rise after a fasted night is not a problem — it's the liver releasing stored glycogen as glucose. A normal adaptive response, not a malfunction.
CGM can tip into paralysis by analysis. Two weeks gives most people actionable information. Wearing one year-round is not the goal — learning your patterns is.
08

Muscle as Metabolic Currency

Strength training is a non-negotiable in Shepherd's framework. Age-related muscle loss is a normal phenomenon; estrogen loss compounds it. Without active resistance training, the trajectory is downward.

Muscle is one of the body's two largest glucose sponges (the other being the brain). Building and preserving muscle directly improves glucose disposal, supports bone density, and — less intuitively — supports brain health. Resistance training has documented cognitive benefits.

What "strength training" actually means here

  • Heavy weight — not light weights-for-tone. The body adapts to load, safely progressed.
  • Short sessions are fine — Shepherd favours focused 30–35 minute workouts over long ones.
  • The "I'll get bulky" fear is misplaced; that is not what happens with progressive resistance training in most women.
  • Cardio remains useful but is not a substitute for resistance work.
Triple return on resistance training: prevents frailty, improves strength and glucose disposal; supports bone density (counters post-menopausal osteoporosis risk); supports brain health and cognitive function.
Muscle is our currency of longevity. You can choose to ignore it — but it will come back to bite you. — Dr. Jessica Shepherd (paraphrased)

Creatine — Not Just for Bodybuilders

Creatine has moved from a gym supplement to a longevity supplement. Evidence is accumulating for brain-barrier crossing effects, cognitive function, and inflammation markers — alongside its established role in muscle.

Common confusion — creatine vs creatinine

Patients often confuse creatine (the supplement) with creatinine (a kidney function marker on lab panels). They are not the same substance. Elevated creatinine is a kidney concern; creatine the supplement is, for most people, safe.

MUSCLE DOSE~5g per day. Shepherd sometimes starts at 2g and titrates up. BRAIN DOSEEmerging data supports ~10g per day for cognitive benefit. WHO SHOULD AVOIDPeople with significant chronic kidney disease. Otherwise broadly well-tolerated.
Creatine's appeal to Shepherd is that it hits multiple systems: muscle building and retention, brain clarity and cognition, and possibly inflammation. Few supplements have that breadth of effect backed by accumulating research.
Standard evidence-based context still applies — get baseline kidney function if there's any history, and start at a lower dose to assess tolerance before moving to the higher brain-health dose.

Morning Routine & Daily Protocol

Cortisol is already elevated in the early morning. The first decisions of the day either compound that stress or settle it. Shepherd's routine is deliberately low-cortisol and stackable within 60 minutes.

MEDITATION10–15 minutes. Shepherd uses guided (Calm app). She calls it a muscle — it builds. SUNLIGHT"Say hello to the sun" — early light exposure plus some vitamin D. WATER + LEMONSimple anti-inflammatory start before any high-glycemic food. PROTEIN FIRSTBreakfast leads with protein, not sugar — keeps energy steady and glucose flat. RESISTANCEGym in the morning, because later in the day it rarely happens. 30–35 minutes, focused.
Shepherd's framing of universal access: if the protocol works for the person with the least resources and the most responsibilities, it reaches everyone. Jumping rope, a 10-minute walk, a bodyweight routine — all count. The gadgets are optional.
Self-care is not selfish. It's the best way to be selfless — you take this time for yourself, then give the rest of your day away. — Gary Brecka (paraphrased)
Brecka's six-year shift: schedule meetings and travel around sleep and exercise, not the other way around. Skip the red-eye; leave the next morning. Small rule, big downstream effect.

Maternal Mortality & the Race Gap

The US leads the developed world in maternal mortality. Black women are 3–4x more likely than other women to die in childbirth. Shepherd's assessment is direct: the causes are systemic, dietary, and communicative — and the discussion as currently held isn't moving the numbers.

The domino chain Shepherd describes

  • Unequal access to resources and quality food in lower-resource communities
  • Diets heavier in inflammatory, processed foods
  • Higher rates of gestational diabetes and cardiovascular disease entering pregnancy
  • Pre-eclampsia — a vascular disorder that reflects that cardiovascular baseline
  • Higher morbidity and mortality in delivery

Shepherd's recommendations for policymakers

  • More women and more people of colour at the decision-making table — you cannot understand a community you are not part of
  • Language that the community being served can actually act on — it's the clinician's job to ensure the patient leaves understanding, not the patient's job to decode
  • Acknowledge systemic racism directly rather than talking around it — it's a precondition for action
Until we take care of a community with the least resources and access, nobody is okay. — Dr. Jessica Shepherd (paraphrased)

Menopause in the Workplace

Many women leave the workforce during perimenopause and menopause — not because they want to, but because symptoms become unmanageable without support. Insurance and workplace policies have not caught up.

Shepherd's parallel: it took roughly 10–15 years for workplaces to accommodate lactating mothers in any systematic way. Midlife women are now at the same starting line — no established norms, no insurance packages tailored to the transition, no time or space to manage symptoms at work.

INSURANCECoverage bundles for labs, HRT, menopause-specialist consultations — largely absent. WORKPLACEAccommodations for hot flashes, cognitive load, time for medical appointments — not standard. RESEARCHWomen were largely excluded from medical research for decades. The assumption that women are "little men" is recent to disprove.
The financial argument for prevention is strong. US healthcare spending is ~$5 trillion annually — more than the next 6–7 countries combined. Shifting a fraction of that into preventive care for midlife women would keep them out of chronic-disease management pathways (thyroid, hypertension, diabetes, autoimmune) that are dramatically more expensive.

It's Never Too Late — A Case Study

Two case studies from the episode illustrate how quickly the body responds to basic inputs — even in frail, older, or post-surgical populations. The lesson is not that intervention is always dramatic; it's that the basics, done consistently, move measurable outcomes fast.

Assisted care / memory care facility

Brecka describes a chain of assisted-living / memory-care facilities that introduced simple, cheap interventions and are tracking outcomes. The changes:

  • Red light therapy, including transcranial red light with a helmet, several times per week
  • Farm-to-table whole foods — processed foods removed from the menu
  • Creatine supplementation
  • Regular sunlight exposure (small gardens, residents wheeled out)

Reported outcomes are subjective so far, but the team is seeing rapid and significant improvement in a heavily-medicated population, many of whom have osteopenia or osteoporosis.

Brecka's mother — 78, post-surgical cognitive crash

After bilateral knee replacement, Brecka's mother was kept in bed for 45 days in a post-surgical rehab facility due to a prosthetic infection. When the family arrived to surprise her, she barely recognised her only child and believed she was being held against her will. The cognitive decline tracked directly with the immobility.

The protocol that reversed it over roughly 10 weeks:

  • Mandated out of bed twice a day, five days a week, for occupational and physical therapy
  • Dietary overhaul — low-glycemic, focused on fats and proteins
  • Four or five targeted supplements including creatine
  • Cerebrolysin (a peptide) for cognitive recovery
  • Hydration and regular sunlight exposure

Brecka's father's check-in call at 10 weeks — she wanted to redecorate the entire house, didn't like any of the artwork. Arguably sharper than before the surgery.

The pattern across both cases: movement, sunlight, nutrition, and a small number of targeted supplements. Low-cost. Low-complexity. Measurable effect in weeks, not years. It is never too late to start, even if starting earlier would have been better.
