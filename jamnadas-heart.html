Heart Disease — How It Actually Happens

Heart disease is now affecting people in their 20s and 30s. The youngest patient Dr. Jamnadas has treated was 28 years old — having a full heart attack with a completely blocked artery. Demographics are shifting: 50% of cardiac patients are now under 65.

The Three-Step Path to a Heart Attack

What Drives Plaque Formation — The Real Causes

  • Insulin — a highly atherogenic (plaque-forming) molecule. Causes smooth muscle proliferation in artery walls, vasoconstriction, increased blood clotting, and inflammation
  • Leaky gut — lipopolysaccharides (LPS) entering the bloodstream trigger immune activation and plaque formation
  • Mould toxicity — causes systemic inflammation that progresses coronary calcium scores
  • Food sensitivities (e.g. undiagnosed coeliac disease) — ongoing gut damage drives inflammation
  • Pesticides, herbicides, PFAS forever chemicals — disrupt metabolism and trigger inflammation
  • Oral microbiome — bad dental hygiene directly linked to valvular heart disease and coronary calcification
  • Chronic sinusitis (especially fungal) — low-grade inflammation linked to premature coronary artery disease
  • Sleep apnoea — causes hypertension and insulin resistance
  • Visceral fat — produces inflammatory molecules IL-6 and TNF that directly promote plaque
Only 20% of patients with significant artery blockages experience chest pain. The other 80% have no warning — which is why screening is critical.
02

Insulin & Visceral Fat

High background insulin — not high blood glucose — is the silent driver of heart disease. Patients can have normal HbA1c and be told they're "not diabetic" while carrying 10 years of damaging hyperinsulinemia. By the time diabetes is diagnosed, coronary artery disease is already present.

Why Glucose Is Toxic & What Insulin Does

  • Glucose in the bloodstream causes glycation — glucose attaches to and damages proteins, enzymes, hormones, and blood vessel walls, accelerating ageing
  • The body responds by releasing insulin to push glucose into cells (liver, muscle, fat)
  • Frequent carbohydrate consumption keeps insulin elevated — insulin stays in the blood for ~4 hours even after glucose has cleared in 2–3
  • Chronically high insulin → insulin resistance → the body needs even more insulin for the same effect
  • By the time diabetes is diagnosed, the patient has already had ~10 years of dangerous hyperinsulinemia
Fasting insulin targetMeasure and aim to keep low — most doctors don't test it but it should be tested Key phenotypeBelly protruding but otherwise not overweight = visceral fat = hyperinsulinemia

Visceral Fat — Why It's Different & Dangerous

  • Visceral fat is created from excess glucose — a different fat to dietary fat. High-calorie diets cause weight gain everywhere; high-glucose diets specifically cause belly visceral fat
  • Visceral fat secretes IL-6 and TNF (tumour necrosis factor) — highly inflammatory molecules that promote plaque formation around the coronary arteries
  • Ectopic fat (fat around the coronary arteries, liver, and pancreas) is now detectable by CT scan and is directly linked to atherosclerosis
  • Biopsy comparison: visceral fat = full of inflammatory molecules; buttock fat = not inflammatory. Two completely different tissues.
  • Fatty liver has three causes: excess alcohol, leaky gut (LPS), and excess insulin converting glucose to fat
After 12 hours of fasting, the body begins pulling fat out of storage — and visceral fat is the first to go. This is why fasting specifically targets the most dangerous fat.

Fasting — Types, Benefits & Protocols

Fasting and calorie restriction are NOT the same thing. Calorie restriction slows metabolism and breaks down muscle. Fasting triggers a completely different physiology — the body draws from fat reserves without sacrificing muscle, produces BDNF, stem cells, and growth hormone, and reverses insulin resistance.

Extended fasting beyond 24–36 hours must be done under medical supervision. During a supervised fast, patients may use water, black tea/coffee, electrolytes (ELEMENT or Celtic salt in water), and MCT oil if hunger is severe.

What Happens During a Fast

  • Hours 0–12: glycogen depleted from liver and muscles
  • After 12 hours: fat mobilisation begins — visceral fat is burned first
  • Insulin drops → fat cells release free fatty acids → liver converts to ketones
  • BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) increases — improved cognition, new cell growth, better reflexes, sharper vision
  • Growth hormone increases — peak growth hormone production occurs during fasting, enabling muscle retention and gain even while fasted
  • Stem cell mobilisation — on breaking the fast, a surge of stem cells from bone marrow enter the bloodstream, becoming immune cells, progenitor cells, and repair cells
  • Endothelial progenitor cells increase — these specifically repair the lining of blood vessels, directly relevant to preventing coronary artery disease
  • Autophagy activates — cells recycle damaged organelles. Mitophagy repairs and renews mitochondria. Toxins are cleared from cells.
  • Immunity boosts — people who fast regularly get fewer infections and colds
Fasting produces real, visible body recomposition. Patients who lose weight through fasting do not get excess loose skin — because fasting preserves and tightens skin in a way calorie restriction does not.

Exercise Timing During a Fast

Exercise at the peak of your fast — 2 hours before breaking the fast. This is when growth hormone is highest, enabling maximum muscle retention and gain even in a fasted state.

  • Resistance training and HIIT are well-tolerated fasted — glucose depletion is not a significant problem
  • Long-duration aerobic exercise (40+ minutes running) fasted is not recommended — this risks muscle breakdown, particularly in women
  • Women: HIIT and resistance training fasted are fine. Sustained treadmill running fasted is not.

Ketones

Ketones are an alternative energy source produced by the liver from fat when insulin is low. They are a cleaner fuel than glucose — producing fewer reactive oxygen species (free radicals) in the mitochondria. They are also signalling molecules.

  • Ketones appear after ~12 hours of fasting (once glycogen is depleted)
  • Produce less oxidative damage to cells than glucose metabolism
  • Trigger BDNF production — new brain cells, sharper cognition, better reflexes
  • Act as signalling molecules that change physiology broadly
  • You are designed to cycle in and out of ketosis — constant ketosis is not the natural state. Fasting and feasting rhythms were normal for human physiology throughout history
Ketogenic diet<50g carbohydrates/day. Used to achieve ketosis without fasting. Cycle in and out — don't stay in permanently. Cycle for weight lossStay ketogenic until goal weight is reached, then return to 18:6 time-restricted eating, with occasional 36-hour fasts monthly.
Dr. Jamnadas: you want both fuel systems (glucose and ketones) working. You are supposed to feast and fast — modern life has removed the fasting half of this cycle entirely.

D3 + K2 — Why They Must Be Taken Together

This is one of the most important supplement combinations in cardiology, yet widely misunderstood.

  • Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption from the gut — ensuring more calcium enters the body
  • Vitamin K2 directs that calcium into bones and teeth — and keeps it OUT of artery walls and soft tissues
  • Without K2, absorbed calcium deposits in the wrong places — including coronary arteries (coronary calcification)
  • Calcium supplements without D3 and K2 increase cardiovascular event risk — calcium goes into arteries, not bones
  • Warfarin (anticoagulant) depletes both K1 and K2 — patients on warfarin have documented higher rates of coronary calcification
Stop all calcium supplements. They increase cardiovascular risk. Fix the underlying cause: D3 deficiency (absorption) and K2 deficiency (direction). Dietary calcium from real food is fine.

Nattokinase — Detail

Nattokinase is an enzyme from natto (fermented soy). Dr. Jamnadas takes it because his own fibrinogen level was at the upper limit of normal — high fibrinogen indicates a greater tendency to form blood clots, a key step in heart attacks.

  • Metabolic syndrome increases clotting tendency and raises fibrinogen
  • Nattokinase mildly reduces blood viscosity without causing the bleeding risks of pharmaceutical anticoagulants
  • No significant bleeding risk at 8,000 units/day

Grass-finished vs grass-fed: "Grass-fed" is often misleading — animals may be fed grass briefly and then finished on corn. Grass-finished means grass from birth to slaughter — this gives more omega-3, more K2, better nutrition, and fewer toxins.

Foods to Avoid — Surprising Ones

  • Excessive fruit — fructose causes fatty liver. Should only be eaten in season, in small amounts. Fruit is not a free food — overconsumption contributes to coronary artery disease, diabetes, and fatty liver
  • White rice — contains arsenic (from contaminated paddyfields). To prepare safely: soak overnight, discard water, cook with excess water, discard again, cool in refrigerator overnight, then reheat. This creates resistant starch (fed to bacteria rather than absorbed) and removes much of the arsenic
  • Orange juice — produces a profound glucose/insulin spike, causes hunger 3–4 hours later. No fibre, high fructose. Not a health food
  • Vegetable seed oils — extremely high in omega-6, distorting the omega-6:omega-3 ratio that is already deeply imbalanced in modern diets
  • Advanced glycation end products (AGEs) — formed by overcooking, blackening, frying food. Nature cannot produce these; only high-temperature cooking does. They cause severe inflammation via RAGE receptors
  • Calcium supplements — increase cardiovascular event risk without D3 and K2 to direct the calcium correctly
  • Excessive B vitamins — particularly from fortified processed foods. May increase fat storage efficiency in mitochondria

Gut Health & the Heart

The biggest border between you and your external environment is not your skin — it is your gut. 100 trillion bacteria and ~400 trillion viruses live there in a symbiotic relationship, producing micronutrients, short-chain fatty acids, postbiotics, and maintaining the integrity of the gut lining.

How Gut Health Drives Heart Disease

  • Leaky gut → LPS (lipopolysaccharides) enter bloodstream → travel to liver → fatty liver → systemic inflammation
  • Dysfunctional microbiome → gut lining integrity compromised → leaky gut → plaque formation
  • Bad oral microbiome → directly linked to valvular heart disease and coronary calcification (unequivocal evidence)
  • Chronic sinusitis (especially fungal) → low-grade systemic inflammation → premature coronary artery disease
  • Dr. Jamnadas case: patient with coronary calcium score of 1,000+ had no conventional risk factors — investigation found a lifelong bad microbiome and chronic constipation (bowel movement every 3–4 days)

Gut Repair Protocol

  • Eat 30–40 different types of vegetables per week — variety matters as different fibres feed different bacterial species
  • Include spices — each counts as a vegetable type
  • Take inulin with FOS powder daily — soluble prebiotic fibre for good bacteria
  • Eat fermented foods daily: kefir (multiple bacterial strains + postbiotics including K2 and SCFAs), kimchi, sauerkraut, yogurt
  • Use spore-based probiotics (Mega Spore) — bypass stomach acid, germinate in the intestines
  • Sleep 7 hours minimum — one night of poor sleep changes gut microbiome composition
  • Fix eating frequency — eating constantly feeds bad bacteria. Time-restricted eating starves them.
Postbiotics are the products made BY gut bacteria — including vitamin K2, short-chain fatty acids, and other beneficial molecules. You get them from fermented foods even if you don't know they're in there.

Toxins & Mould

Toxins are a major underappreciated driver of cardiovascular disease. Dr. Jamnadas tests all patients with blood, urine, and stool samples for detoxification ability, heavy metals, pesticides, herbicides, and mould. He is finding unexpectedly high levels across his patient population.

Mould — An Emerging Concern

  • ~70% of homes have some form of mould toxicity — from water damage or exposure
  • Mould triggers the innate immune system → chronic low-grade inflammation → progressive coronary artery disease
  • When Dr. Jamnadas removes mould from patients' environments, the rate of coronary calcium score progression flattens
  • Mould can colonise the gut and sinuses — recontaminating the person even after leaving the mouldy environment
  • Fungal sinusitis is directly linked to premature coronary artery disease
  • Blood markers of mould: elevated CRP, complement levels, and specific inflammatory molecule ratios
If you smell an earthy, pungent smell near cracks or seams in wet areas (bathrooms, steam rooms, basements) — that is almost certainly mould. Smell directly near the crack. If the smell intensifies, arrange professional remediation. Do not ignore it.

Other Toxins

  • Arsenic in rice — from contaminated paddy fields. Slow poison at chronic low doses. Mitigation: soak overnight, discard water, cook in excess water, discard again, refrigerate, reheat (creates resistant starch)
  • Pesticides and herbicides — oestrogen receptor disruptors. Living on or near a golf course (heavily treated) is a risk factor. Buy organic produce.
  • PFAS / BPA — disrupt metabolism, cause inflammation. Avoid plastic containers and non-stick cookware
  • Heavy metals — found in contaminated soil and water. The liver detoxifies these but can be overwhelmed. Healthy gut bacteria help excrete heavy metals in stool.
  • AGEs (Advanced Glycation End Products) — produced by blackening, burning, or frying food. Cause severe inflammation. Cook at lower temperatures; avoid charred food.

Exercise for Heart Health

Excessive aerobic exercise causes more coronary artery disease than short sprints and resistance training. This contradicts conventional wisdom and is based on Dr. Jamnadas's direct clinical observation across 250,000+ patients.

Recommended Exercise

  • HIIT: 30–45 seconds intense work, 30–45 seconds complete rest — allows the body to clear reactive oxygen species between bursts
  • Resistance / body-weight exercises: planks, leg lifts, push-ups, flow exercises
  • Short aerobic sessions: 15–20 minutes only — for cardiovascular endurance
  • Exercise at peak of fast (2 hours before breaking it) for maximum growth hormone

What to Avoid

  • Excessive endurance aerobic training: 2-hour daily treadmill runs, 100-mile cycling days, marathon training every day
  • This level of aerobic exercise creates more inflammation and more coronary artery disease than patients who do only short sprints and resistance work
  • Over-exercise diverts blood from the gut → gut ischaemia → vagus nerve dysfunction → heart palpitations and arrhythmias
The rate-limiting step in cleaning up oxidative stress from exercise is glutathione and superoxide dismutase production. HIIT with rest intervals allows this cleanup to occur between bursts — continuous aerobic exercise does not give the body time to catch up.

The Vagus Nerve

The vagus nerve is the largest nerve in the body. Its greatest distribution is in the gut — reflecting how important gut health is to the brain. It carries bidirectional information between the gut, heart, lungs, and brain. A dysfunctional vagus nerve = too much sympathetic (fight-or-flight), too little parasympathetic (repair).

Benefits of a Healthy Vagus Nerve

  • Faster healing and recovery
  • Reduced coronary artery disease progression
  • Lower blood pressure
  • Less blood clotting tendency
  • Lower inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF, CRP, small dense LDL)
  • Better heart rate variability (HRV) — when Dr. Jamnadas fixes patients' guts, their HRV improves
  • Resolution of POTS (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome) in young women — often caused by gut dysfunction

How to Stimulate the Vagus Nerve

  • Breathing exercise — inhale for count of 4, exhale for count of 8. Deliberately long expiration. 10 minutes daily stimulates parasympathetic nerve endings.
  • Cold water / ice pack on front of neck — vagus nerve lies close to the carotid sheath. Cold stimulates it. Ice pack on back of neck also works.
  • Eye movements — looking right, left, up, down stimulates the vagus nerve via connections to the oculomotor nerve
  • Eyeball pressure — gentle cold water on closed eyes triggers the dive reflex, lowering pulse rate
  • Humming / singing for 10 minutes — vibration is transmitted to the vagus nerve, creates electrical impulses
  • Laughing — diaphragmatic movement stimulates the vagus nerve. Laughing clubs exist for this purpose.
  • Valsalva manoeuvre — breathe in, then bear down without releasing the air. Compresses the thoracic and abdominal cavity, stimulates vagus nerve
  • Fix the gut — the primary, most lasting intervention. Vagus nerve has its largest distribution in the gut lining. Healing the gut heals the vagus nerve.
  • Omega-3 supplementation — DHA specifically is required for optimal vagus nerve function

LDL, Statins & Cholesterol

Total LDL is not the problem. Small, dense, damaged LDL is the problem. This distinction changes everything about how cardiovascular risk should be assessed and managed.

LDL — What Actually Matters

  • Large, fluffy LDL particles — normal, not atherogenic. Even high levels are not a problem.
  • Small, dense, oxidised LDL — these destroy the glycocalyx (artery lining), activate endothelium, get engulfed by macrophages, become foam cells, and form plaque
  • Recent data: patients with the highest LDL levels live into their 90s and have healthier lives — it is the damaged LDL, not total LDL, that matters
Five causes of small dense LDL1. Sugar/glucose 2. Omega-6 oils 3. AGEs (overcooked food) 4. Toxins 5. LPS from leaky gut Test to requestLDL subfraction test — is your LDL large and fluffy or small and dense? Available in the US; harder to access in some other countries

Statins — Honest Assessment

  • Statins lower LDL — but lowering total LDL is not the same as reducing cardiovascular events
  • 20–30% of patients experience myopathy (muscle weakness, aches, pains)
  • Statins can cause diabetes by inducing mitochondrial dysfunction
  • Can cause cognitive impairment — memory problems, brain fog. Often reverses when statins are stopped
  • Can cause liver dysfunction — requires monitoring
  • The goal should be eliminating small dense LDL — not simply lowering total LDL with a drug
Statins are not universally safe. If you are on a statin and experiencing muscle weakness, memory problems, or have developed diabetes since starting, discuss statin-related side effects with your doctor. Stopping without medical advice is not recommended.

Testing & Screening

Two tests that every adult over 30 should have. These are Dr. Jamnadas's recommendations — not standard NHS/healthcare guidance.

1. Coronary Calcium Score (CT Scan)

A CT scan that measures calcium deposits in coronary artery walls. Calcium = plaque = atherosclerosis. The more calcium, the more plaque present.

Score of 0No coronary calcium — you are in a good place. Continue prevention. Score >0Atherosclerosis is already present. See a prevention-focused cardiologist immediately. TrackingUsed to monitor treatment — the score should not be rising. If it is, something is still driving inflammation. Dr. J investigates until he finds the cause. Who needs itAll adults over 30 with any concerns about heart health or family history

2. Inflammatory Blood Panel (Cleveland Heart Labs)

Standard cholesterol panels are insufficient. Dr. Jamnadas orders the Cleveland Heart Labs panel which goes much deeper.

  • HDL, LDL, cholesterol — with particle size analysis (small dense vs large fluffy)
  • Is the LDL oxidised?
  • CRP (C-reactive protein) — general inflammation marker
  • IL-6 (interleukin-6) — inflammatory cytokine
  • TNF (tumour necrosis factor) — inflammatory cytokine
  • HbA1c — long-term blood glucose average
  • Fasting insulin level — the most important number most doctors don't test
Inflammation is invisible — you cannot feel it in most cases. The blood test is the only way to know. Mental fog, low mood, poor memory, and poor sleep are symptoms of systemic inflammation — not just lifestyle issues.

Warning Signs & Symptoms

Signs of cardiovascular risk and systemic inflammation that can be observed or felt — and what they indicate.

Physical Signs

  • Protruding belly — the single most visible indicator of visceral fat and hyperinsulinemia. "If you have a belly, you have a problem."
  • Joint pain — often inflammation-driven, not just mechanical. When gut and diet are fixed, joint pain typically resolves
  • Bad breath — linked to oral and gut microbiome dysfunction, which correlates with cardiovascular risk
  • Chronic sinusitis / blocked nose / sinus headaches — possible fungal mould exposure; directly linked to coronary disease
  • Heart palpitations — especially in otherwise healthy people, often indicates vagus nerve dysfunction from gut issues or sympathetic overdrive from stress/caffeine
  • POTS (rapid heartbeat on standing) in young women — often a gut problem, not a heart problem

Mental & Cognitive Signs

  • Brain fog / difficulty concentrating — a symptom of systemic inflammation
  • Depression — inflammation in the brain. Not purely psychological. Often resolves with anti-inflammatory diet.
  • Memory decline — inflammation-driven. Also a symptom of statin side effects in some.
  • Feeling suboptimal / not at your best — pay attention to this. Inflammation does not always have a visible physical marker.
If you have chest pain, severe breathlessness, sudden crushing pressure across the chest, or pain radiating to the jaw or left arm — call emergency services immediately. Do not attempt to drive yourself to hospital.
