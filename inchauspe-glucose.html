The 10 Glucose Hacks
Reference Guide

Biochemist and bestselling author of Glucose Revolution and The Glucose Goddess Method. This guide covers the biology of glucose spikes, her 10 dietary hacks, the 2,700-person experiment, and the links Inchauspé draws between glucose and aging, fertility, mental health, menopause and Alzheimer's.

How to read this guide

A short orientation. Inchauspé is a biochemist with a Master's degree, not a doctor or registered dietitian, and she sells a supplement (Anti-Spike Formula) that she discusses on the podcast. Most of her core dietary advice — eat protein at breakfast, eat fibre before refined carbs, walk after meals, prefer whole fruit over juice — aligns with mainstream nutrition guidance. A few claims in the conversation are more contested or commercially motivated, and those are flagged inline throughout this guide with red caution boxes.

The hacks themselves are uncontroversial as a category — they are practical applications of the well-known idea that reducing post-meal blood glucose excursions is generally beneficial, especially for people with insulin resistance, prediabetes or type 2 diabetes. Where Inchauspé extends those mechanisms to claims about Alzheimer's, PCOS reversal, fertility, and her own supplement, the evidence is thinner than the confident framing suggests. Those points are flagged where they appear.

02

Why glucose spikes matter

Inchauspé says one billion people worldwide have type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, and that even among people with no diagnosed metabolic condition, up to 80% experience daily glucose spikes. She frames spikes as the trigger for three damaging downstream processes.

Two food categories raise blood glucose: starches (bread, rice, pasta, potatoes, oats) and sugars (anything sweet, including fruit and juice). Both are made of glucose molecules. Eat a lot at once and a lot of glucose hits the blood quickly — the spike. A few small spikes are not a problem; large repeated spikes are.

1. Mitochondrial overload

Mitochondria are cellular factories that turn glucose into energy. Inchauspé's analogy: mitochondria are like a houseplant — some glucose gives steady energy, too much glucose at once and they "drown." When overwhelmed, they release free radicals which drive inflammation. The dopamine hit from sugary food can feel like an energy boost but is a pleasure response, not real energy production.

2. Glycation — "cooking" from the inside

Glycation is the same chemical process that browns a chicken in the oven: glucose molecules bind to proteins. Babies' cartilage is white; 90-year-olds' cartilage is brown. Every glucose spike accelerates this process throughout the body, contributing to wrinkles externally and organ damage internally. This is why "advanced glycation end-products" (AGEs) appear in skincare science and aging research.

3. Insulin release and fat storage

In response to a spike, the pancreas releases insulin, which clears excess glucose into the liver and muscles first, then into fat cells once those stores are full. Repeated large insulin releases drive insulin resistance, the precursor to type 2 diabetes. Inchauspé notes: when insulin is high, the body cannot burn fat — insulin is a storage hormone.

Some glucose: amazing, steady energy. Too much glucose, and your mitochondria start freaking out — the same way a plant drowns if you overwater it. — Jessie Inchauspé

The four headline hacks

These four are the ones Inchauspé tested in her 2,700-person experiment and recommends as the highest-leverage starting point. Her instruction: don't change anything else about what you eat — just add these in.

Hack 1 — Savoury breakfast

Inchauspé calls this the single most important hack — if you only do one, do this. A sweet breakfast (cereal, juice, granola, fruit smoothie, pastries) creates a glucose spike that sets up a roller-coaster for the rest of the day: spike, crash, cravings, repeat. A savoury, protein-heavy breakfast keeps glucose steady and should keep you full for around four hours.

PROTEINEggs, fish, meat, tofu, nuts, dairy, protein powder, dinner leftovers FATSOlive oil, butter, avocado OPTIONALA piece of bread or some potatoes for taste AVOIDCereal, muffins, orange juice, granola, fruit purée — and no sugar in coffee
If you intermittent fast, the rule still applies: whatever you break the fast with should be savoury. The empty digestive system is more sensitive, so a fast broken with sugar produces a particularly large spike.

Hack 2 — Vinegar before meals

One tablespoon of vinegar in a large glass of water, drunk roughly 10 minutes before the biggest meal of the day. Inchauspé claims this reduces the glucose spike of the meal by up to 30%, and the insulin spike by a similar amount. The mechanism: acetic acid temporarily inactivates the digestive enzymes that break starches and sugars into individual glucose molecules, slowing absorption.

DOSE1 tablespoon, diluted in a large glass of water TIMING~10 minutes before the meal TYPEAny kitchen vinegar — white, apple cider, red wine. Not cleaning vinegar. AVOIDBranded "vinegar drinks" with added sugar or fruit juice
Always dilute. Undiluted vinegar can damage tooth enamel and irritate the oesophagus. Inchauspé is explicit about this in the conversation. People on insulin or sulfonylureas for diabetes should speak to their doctor before adding vinegar, as compounding the glucose-lowering effect can risk hypoglycaemia.
Inchauspé says her broader claim — vinegar's effects on visceral fat, cholesterol, glucose and insulin — is supported by clinical literature. The 30% spike-reduction figure is a ceiling drawn from small studies; results in any individual will vary.

Hack 3 — Veggie starters

Eat a small plate of vegetables before the rest of the meal — Italian antipasti, French crudités, Middle Eastern herbs by the bunch. The fibre coats the intestine, forming a mesh that slows glucose absorption from anything eaten afterwards. Inchauspé's point: this traditional habit exists across cultures for a reason, and the science now explains why.

Practical examples: a small bowl of cherry tomatoes, raw carrots, broccoli, or any salad with a vinegar-based dressing (which combines hacks 2 and 3 in one). Order matters more than quantity.

Hack 4 — Move after eating

Within 90 minutes of finishing a meal, use your muscles for around 10 minutes. Walk, clean, dance — or, if stuck at a desk, do calf raises (push up onto the balls of your feet, repeatedly). The calves contain the soleus muscle, which is unusually effective at soaking up glucose from the blood.

The mechanism: muscle contraction triggers insulin-independent glucose uptake into the muscle cells, blunting the post-meal spike. This is one of the better-evidenced hacks in the list — the soleus pushup specifically has been studied and shown to lower post-prandial glucose.

If you have to eat sugar, the best time is right before or right after a workout — your muscles are hungry for glucose and will absorb it directly rather than allowing a large spike.
04

The other six hacks

Beyond the headline four, Inchauspé references the rest of her ten in The Glucose Goddess Method. In this conversation she walks through five additional ones (the tenth is in the book but not separately named in the podcast). They are smaller-effect or more situational than the headline four, but follow the same logic: slow glucose arrival, blunt insulin response.

HACK 05Eat in the right order. Vegetables first, then proteins and fats, then starches and sugars. Same meal, same quantity — different order can reduce the spike by up to 75%. HACK 06Clothes on carbs. Never eat starch or sugar naked. Pair bread with avocado, cake with Greek yoghurt — fat, protein or fibre as a "clothing" slows absorption. HACK 07Savoury snacks. When snacking between meals on an empty stomach, choose savoury over sweet. HACK 08Sweet only as dessert. If you want cake, have it after lunch or dinner — never alone or first thing. The food already in your gut blunts the spike. HACK 09Whole fruit only. Never juiced, dried or smoothied. Smoothing breaks the fibre. Juice is "drinking sugar water."
The "up to 75%" figure for food order comes from a small Cornell study (Shukla et al., 2015) of 11 people with type 2 diabetes. The directional finding — fibre and protein before starch reduces the spike — is well-supported. The headline percentage is the best case from a tiny sample and shouldn't be treated as a typical result.
05

The 2,700-person experiment

In 2019, Inchauspé recruited 2,700 people from 110 countries via Instagram. They followed a four-week protocol, adding one hack per week. She published the self-reported results in The Glucose Goddess Method.

WEEK 1Savoury breakfast every day WEEK 2Add vinegar once a day WEEK 3Add veggie starters WEEK 4Add movement after eating

Self-reported headline results (her figures):

  • 90% reduced their cravings
  • 90% had more energy
  • Significant reported improvements in sleep, hormones, skin and mood
  • Of those who wanted to lose weight, ~40% did, with no calorie restriction
This is not a clinical trial. There was no control group, no placebo, no blinding, and participants self-selected from Inchauspé's Instagram following — a population already motivated to believe and to report improvement. Inchauspé acknowledges this in the conversation. The numbers are interesting as a customer-satisfaction signal but cannot establish that the hacks caused the outcomes. Don't read "90% reduced cravings" as comparable to a clinical efficacy figure.
06

Sweeteners, sugar and fruit

Inchauspé's central message: there is no such thing as "good sugar." A fruit smoothie and a chocolate cake contain chemically identical sucrose; the body does not distinguish source. Honey, agave, maple syrup all carry health halos but are the same molecules.

Glucose vs fructose vs starch

Glucose itself isn't sweet — what tastes sweet in fruit is fructose. Sucrose (table sugar) is half glucose, half fructose. Starches (bread, pasta, rice, potatoes) are long chains of glucose linked together; they don't taste sweet but break down into pure glucose on digestion. So a piece of bread can produce as big a spike as a piece of cake.

Sweeteners — Inchauspé's tier list

FINEStevia, monk fruit, allulose CONCERNSAspartame, sucralose HER VIEWDiet soda is "always" better than full-sugar soda
Inchauspé's "diet soda is always better than regular" framing overstates current scientific consensus. In 2023, the WHO's IARC classified aspartame as "possibly carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2B), and the WHO separately advised against using non-sugar sweeteners for weight control, noting they don't appear to help long-term and may carry cardiovascular and metabolic risks. A 2025 NIH-funded study linked daily aspartame intake to faster cognitive decline. The defensible position is "neither is a health food — water is the answer" rather than "always pick diet."

Fruit — what's changed

Modern fruit has been bred for thousands of years for sweetness, juiciness and seedlessness — Inchauspé's analogy is the way wolves were bred into chihuahuas. Grapes, bananas, mangoes and papayas are particularly high in sugar. Berries are lower.

Whole fruit is fine because the fibre is intact and slows absorption. The problem is denaturing fruit — juicing removes the fibre and leaves sugar water; smoothies break the fibre structure even when it's still present.

The whole-fruit point is well-supported. Across large epidemiological studies, whole-fruit consumption is consistently associated with better health outcomes (lower diabetes risk, lower cardiovascular mortality), while fruit juice is associated with the opposite. The fibre-and-matrix hypothesis is the leading explanation.
Orange juice comes from oranges, therefore it must be good for you. What people don't realise is the sugar in a can of Coke is also natural — it comes from sugar beets or canes. They all come from plants. What matters is the concentration and the medium. — Jessie Inchauspé
07

Downstream effects: skin, fertility, hormones

Inchauspé argues the same mechanism — repeated spikes producing inflammation, glycation and elevated insulin — explains a wide range of health conditions. Some of these links are well-established; others are speculative extensions.

Skin and aging

Wrinkles: glycation contributes by damaging collagen and elastin. Many skincare products target glycation externally with antioxidants; reducing glucose spikes targets it internally. Don't smoke, wear sunscreen, and reduce glucose spikes — in roughly that order of impact.

Inflammatory skin conditions (eczema, psoriasis, acne, rosacea): glucose spikes drive inflammation through three mechanisms simultaneously — mitochondrial stress, glycation itself, and high insulin. Cutting spikes can reduce flare-up frequency and intensity in those susceptible.

PCOS and fertility

Inchauspé says higher insulin resistance correlates with higher infertility, and that around 60% of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) cases involve insulin resistance. The mechanism: high insulin signals the ovaries to produce more testosterone, which can cause acne, facial hair, balding and missed periods.

Her practical point: the birth control pill masks the symptoms of high testosterone by adding female hormones, but doesn't address the underlying insulin issue. When women come off the pill to try to conceive, the high testosterone is still there.

Inchauspé describes PCOS as "not a real condition — it's a cluster of symptoms." This framing is misleading. PCOS is a defined endocrine disorder with international diagnostic criteria (the Rotterdam criteria), recognised by the WHO, NHS, NIH and major endocrine societies. The insulin-resistant subtype is real and well-documented, and lifestyle interventions including the kind of dietary changes Inchauspé recommends are part of standard care. But not all PCOS is insulin-driven — the lean PCOS phenotype exists, and presenting "do my hacks" as a general PCOS solution overstates the evidence. Anyone with PCOS should work with an endocrinologist or gynaecologist, not rely on dietary hacks alone. The anecdote of three women in Inchauspé's program getting pregnant in a month is encouraging but not a controlled study.

Menopause

After menopause, glucose response to the same food becomes higher — the body's ability to manage glucose declines. Inchauspé notes studies showing that reducing glucose spikes during menopause provides some symptom relief, including for insomnia and hot flushes. Diet is one lever among several; this is not a substitute for HRT for those who choose it.

08

Mental health, mood, the brain

This is the angle that drew Inchauspé into the field — she had a personal history of depersonalisation episodes she traces to glucose dysregulation. She argues for tighter links between glucose and mental state than most clinicians yet accept.

Brain fog and "food coma"

Studies in rats show that frequent glucose spikes slow the speed of neural signalling. Researchers have theorised this maps to brain fog in humans. Inchauspé reports that participants in her experiment had significantly less post-meal fatigue at 90 minutes — the classic food-coma window. Cognitive performance on tasks is measurably worse after a high-glucose breakfast than after a steady one.

Depression and insulin resistance

A 10-year prospective study followed 300 people with no insulin resistance and no depression at baseline. Those who developed insulin resistance during the decade had nearly a twofold higher rate of subsequently developing major depression. Correlation, not causation — but consistent with a growing literature linking metabolic and mental health.

The voodoo doll marriage study

Researchers gave 300 married couples voodoo dolls of their spouse and asked them to insert a pin every time the spouse annoyed them, for six weeks. Glucose was measured throughout. People with more variable glucose — particularly more low-glucose events following spikes — put more pins in. Researchers speculated the link is via tyrosine, a neurotransmitter precursor that regulates mood and that depletes on the glucose roller-coaster.

This is the well-known Bushman et al. (2014) "Low glucose relates to greater aggression in married couples" study published in PNAS. The finding is real; the causal chain is plausible but not proven.

Alzheimer's — "type 3 diabetes"

A 30-year study found that elevated glucose levels in people's 40s correlated with higher rates of Alzheimer's disease in their 60s, 70s and 80s. The Alzheimer's brain shows three of the same hallmarks as the diabetic body: inflammation, glycation, and insulin resistance. Some researchers now describe Alzheimer's as "type 3 diabetes" — diabetes of the brain.

The "type 3 diabetes" framing is a real hypothesis in the literature — most associated with Suzanne de la Monte's research from around 2008 onwards — but it remains contested and is not mainstream Alzheimer's consensus. Most Alzheimer's researchers regard amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation and vascular contributions as the dominant frameworks, with insulin resistance as one contributing factor among many. The correlation between midlife metabolic health and later cognitive decline is well-supported; the unified "type 3 diabetes" model is not.
09

GLP-1 drugs and what they replace

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a hormone the body produces naturally. Drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) deliver doses many times higher than the body normally produces.

How natural GLP-1 works

L-cells in the digestive tract sense incoming food and release GLP-1. The hormone signals satiety to the brain (telling you to stop eating) and helps the body manage incoming glucose by directing it into liver, muscle and fat stores. It's a normal part of post-meal physiology.

The drug versions

Pharmaceutical GLP-1 agonists deliver 10–100x natural levels. Result: glucose drops sharply, appetite is largely zapped, weight comes off. First approved for type 2 diabetes, now used widely for weight loss.

CONCERN 1Up to 40% of weight lost is muscle mass, not fat CONCERN 2When people stop, they regain ~70% of lost weight on average — and the regain is mostly fat MITIGATIONIf on these drugs: weight train 3x/week, eat plenty of protein
Inchauspé's broader frame: the existence of these drugs as a public-health response is itself the story. We've engineered a drug to stop people eating the food around them, rather than fixing the food. Her analogy: "as if we found out that tap water was toxic and instead of fixing the tap water we invented a drug that made you less thirsty."

Inchauspé's Anti-Spike Formula

Inchauspé sells a supplement called Anti-Spike Formula and discusses it on the podcast. She describes the two active ingredients and the claimed mechanisms.

INGREDIENT 1Lemon extract — claimed to support natural GLP-1 production INGREDIENT 2Mulberry leaf extract (containing DNJ, 1-deoxynojirimycin) — claimed to inhibit the digestive enzymes that break starch into glucose, similar mechanism to vinegar CLAIMReduces glucose spike of carbs and sugar by up to 40%
This is a commercial product Inchauspé sells. Conflict of interest is significant and should weigh heavily in how the claims are received. The mulberry leaf / DNJ research is real but small-scale — alpha-glucosidase inhibition is a known mechanism (it's the same mechanism as the prescription diabetes drug acarbose). The "up to 40%" figure is a best-case from limited trials. There are no large independent studies of this specific formulation. By Inchauspé's own logic in the same conversation, the food hacks (vinegar, veggies first, savoury breakfast) deliver similar effects for free.
People taking diabetes medication, particularly insulin or sulfonylureas, should not add glucose-lowering supplements without speaking to their prescriber — the effects can stack and risk hypoglycaemia.

Calories, fasting, kids, coffee

A grab bag of related questions from the conversation.

Calories

Inchauspé's framing: the calorie is a measurement of heat released by burning food in a box of water. Two foods with identical calorie counts can produce very different metabolic responses. A 2,000-calorie diet of avocados leaves a person steady; a 2,000-calorie diet of glucose-spiking foods produces hunger, cravings and brain fog.

Energy balance — calories in vs calories out — still governs weight change at the bottom line. Inchauspé's point that food quality affects hunger, satiety and metabolic health is correct. Where the framing slips is implying calories are misleading or unimportant; they're not. Two diets of identical calories will differ enormously in how easy they are to sustain and in their effect on body composition and health, but a sustained calorie surplus will still cause weight gain regardless of glucose response.

Fasting

Inchauspé says intermittent fasting works better in men than in women, and that for women it can disrupt hormones if used aggressively. Fasting is a stressor on the body — a useful one in moderation, but it stacks with other stressors (intense work, kids, coffee, HIIT, sauna, cold plunge). She fasts mainly on holiday and skips meals occasionally rather than running rigid daily windows.

When breaking a fast, the rule of savoury-first applies more strongly. The empty digestive system absorbs incoming sugar more rapidly, producing a particularly large spike if the first food is sweet.

Children and sugar

Type 2 diabetes used to be an adult disease and is now appearing in 5-year-olds. Inchauspé is particularly opposed to giving children orange juice and sweet cereals, framing them as effectively dessert. Her view: if a child is begging for sugary cereal, that's not a reason to provide it — the analogy she uses is whether you'd give them cigarettes if they asked. Children adapt to what's available at home; she grew up with no soda in the house and never developed a taste for it.

Coffee

In some people, even black coffee can cause a small glucose spike via the cortisol/stress response — particularly if the drinker feels jittery or anxious from caffeine. If you slept badly, drinking your coffee after breakfast rather than before can reduce its glucose effect by up to 50%.

Inchauspé's broader take: coffee studies consistently show coffee drinkers have fewer diseases on average. The downside, where there is one, is mostly what people put in it — sugar and oat milk being the worst offenders. (Her view on oat milk: "pasta juice" — i.e., starch in a glass.)

A practical daily template

Pulling the conversation into one practical day, in the order Inchauspé would prioritise.

ON WAKINGIf you slept badly, delay coffee until after breakfast. Otherwise, black coffee is fine — no sugar, no oat milk. BREAKFASTSavoury, protein-led: eggs, leftovers, fish, Greek yoghurt, avocado on bread. Nothing sweet — no juice, no cereal, no granola, no fruit purée. Aim to stay full for 4 hours. MID-MORNINGIf hungry: savoury snack (nuts, cheese, hummus and veg). Not fruit alone, not biscuits. BEFORE LUNCH1 tbsp vinegar in a large glass of water, ~10 min before eating. LUNCHVeg/salad first, then protein and fat, then any starch. If carbs are present, pair them with fat or protein — never naked. POST-LUNCH10 minutes of movement within 90 min — a walk, calf raises at the desk, light errands. DINNERSame order rule: veg first, then protein, then carbs. Avoid sugar before bed — it disrupts deep sleep. DESSERTIf you want it, have it after the meal, not as a standalone snack.
The Inchauspé position in one sentence: she is not anti-sugar, she is anti-spike. Her hacks let people keep eating what they enjoy while flattening the curve.
