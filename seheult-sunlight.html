Board-certified pulmonologist and critical care intensivist, sleep physician, and co-founder of MedCram. The bulk of the conversation is the case for sunlight, infrared light and mitochondrial health.

01

The Eight Pillars — NEWSTART

Seheult frames health as a chain of links — heart, lungs, liver, kidneys and so on. Disease shows up where the weakest link breaks. Modern medicine treats the broken link with drugs, but every drug has side effects, so you're effectively leveraging other links to protect the failing one. The longevity strategy is the opposite: strengthen all the links upstream so nothing breaks. Eight categories of intervention do this. Together they spell NEWSTART, a framework codified by Weimar University in Northern California.

E · EXERCISE

All medications have side effects. The aim is to use that knowledge to leverage the other links to protect the weakest one. Exercise has side benefits — it improves every link at once. — Roger Seheult

The premise running through every pillar: chronic disease is downstream of mitochondrial dysfunction. By the time we're old, the energy output of our cellular batteries has dropped roughly 70%. Where that drop bites depends on the tissue — fatty liver in the liver, congestive heart failure in the heart, dementia in the brain, blood sugar dysregulation in metabolic tissue. The interventions in NEWSTART are the ones that protect, repair or recharge mitochondria.

02

Sunlight ≠ Vitamin D

The single biggest misconception, in Seheult's view: that sunlight equals vitamin D, and therefore a vitamin D capsule replaces going outside. It doesn't. Vitamin D supplementation is real and useful — but it's a fraction of what sunlight does. The active ingredient he focuses on is infrared light, and its target is the mitochondria.

Sunlight is a broad spectrum running from ultraviolet B at the short-wavelength end (the part that makes vitamin D in skin) to far infrared at the long-wavelength end. The key physical principle: longer wavelengths penetrate further. Just as bass from a car next to you sounds muffled and travels through the chassis, infrared travels through skin and clothes — up to roughly 8 cm into tissue, according to light engineer Scott Zimmerman.

UVBShortest biological wavelength. Makes vitamin D in the skin. Cannot penetrate clothes or window glass well. Blocked obliquely by atmosphere when sun is low. VISIBLEWhat you see. Includes blue light, which signals "daytime" to your circadian system via the retina. REDToward the long end of visible. Penetrates several cm. Activates mitochondria. The wavelength used in red-light therapy devices. INFRAREDInvisible. Penetrates ~8 cm. Goes through clothes. The main driver of mitochondrial melatonin. Trees reflect it; concrete doesn't.

Crucially, in winter at northern latitudes (UK included), the sun is too low in the sky for UVB to make it through atmospheric scattering — so you can't make vitamin D from sun for several months. But infrared still gets through. So even when "vitamin D season" is closed, the sunlight benefit isn't.

When you're outside on a clear day you get the entire biological spectrum — UV, visible, red and infrared — at the right relative intensities. No artificial source replicates this. Lamps, windows and screens give you slices.

Modern windows in particular — especially in hot climates like Southern California — are often engineered to block infrared specifically, because infrared is what heats a building. The unintended consequence: even sitting by a sunny window indoors, the infrared component is largely filtered out.

03

Mitochondria, Melatonin & the Cooling System

The central mechanism in Seheult's argument. Mitochondrial output drops about 70% across a lifetime. Just as a car engine produces both motion and heat, mitochondria produce both ATP and oxidative stress — and oxidative stress, unmanaged, is what destroys them. The cell's cooling system for that oxidative stress is melatonin, but not the kind you swallow.

PINEAL MELATONINMade in the brain at night. Circulates in blood. Tells the body it's time to sleep. About 5% of the body's melatonin. MITOCHONDRIAL MELATONINMade inside the mitochondria of every cell. Stays local. A powerful antioxidant that protects against oxidative stress. The remaining ~95%.

The 2019 paper by Russell Reiter (executive editor of Melatonin Research, University of Texas) and light engineer Scott Zimmerman proposed that infrared light hitting the body penetrates deep enough to stimulate and upregulate mitochondrial melatonin production. Result: less oxidative stress, healthier mitochondria, more energy output, and downstream protection against the diseases that follow from mitochondrial dysfunction — dementia, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, kidney disease.

THE MITOCHONDRIAL COOLING LOOP

Seheult's COVID-era epiphany came from this framework. The patients dying in his ICU weren't the predicted respiratory cases — they were people with obesity, dementia, diabetes, heart failure, kidney disease. All chronic conditions rooted in mitochondrial dysfunction. SARS-CoV-2 binds the ACE2 receptor, which itself plays a role in mitigating oxidative stress. So the virus knocks out part of every cell's cooling system. Patients whose mitochondrial cooling was already marginal couldn't make it over the additional hill the virus imposed.

Picture driving up a hill. Who makes it over? The engines that were running cool to begin with. The ones with the temperature gauge already in the red are the ones pulled over with steam coming out of the hood. — Roger Seheult on COVID severity
04

The Case for Sun — Studies, Anecdotes, Magnitude

The clinical and epidemiological case Seheult assembles. He acknowledges most of these are association studies, not randomised trials, but stacks them via the Bradford Hill criteria — the same logic that established smoking causes lung cancer.

The Swedish women's cohort (Lindqvist et al., ~30,000 women, 20 years)

Roughly 20–30,000 Swedish women, surveyed about sun habits, divided into low / moderate / high sun exposure groups, followed for 20 years. The women with the most sun exposure had the lowest mortality from cancer, cardiovascular disease and non-cardiovascular disease. Magnitude: women in the low-sun group who didn't smoke had the same all-cause mortality as women in the high-sun group who did smoke. Avoiding the sun, in this dataset, looked like the same risk factor as smoking.

UK Biobank (Richard Weller, Edinburgh) — ~10× larger replication

Dermatologist Richard Weller did a similar study an order of magnitude larger — roughly 300–400,000 people, both sexes — using UK Biobank data plus solar radiation by region of residence. Same finding: more sun → lower all-cause and non-skin cancer mortality. Critically, no statistically increased melanoma incidence. Weller's op-ed in the Journal of Investigative Dermatology ("Sunlight: time for a rethink") argues public health bodies that frame the sun as "a deadly laser to be avoided at all costs" are getting the trade-off wrong.

The Brazilian infrared jacket trial (n=30, hospitalised COVID)

A custom jacket lined with LEDs emitting at 940 nm (infrared). Hospitalised COVID patients sick enough to be admitted but not in ICU. Randomised, blinded (light invisible, no heat). 15 minutes a day for 7 days. Every endpoint reached statistical significance: oxygen saturation, breath strength, white cell counts, heart rate, respiratory rate. Length of stay dropped from 12 days (sham) to 8 days (active) — a 4-day reduction on a small sample.

The Green Heart study (South Louisville, Kentucky)

Researchers measured high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP, a marker of inflammation linked to stroke and heart attack risk) in roughly 700 people across a four-square-mile urbanised area. They then planted 8,000 mature trees into the area. Two years later, hs-CRP had dropped 13–20% in the same residents — corresponding to an estimated 10–15% reduction in stroke risk. No socioeconomic change, no exercise programme. Hard to attribute to anything other than the trees themselves.

Latitude predicts COVID surges

Researchers asked what predicted when European countries got their first autumn 2020 surge. Not temperature. Not humidity. Latitude. The wave moved north to south as the sun's angle dropped — Finland first, Greece last. A separate study from the University of Edinburgh, focused on the northern US in winter (where vitamin D synthesis is impossible regardless of behaviour), found regions with more winter sunlight had lower COVID mortality. Replicated in England and Italy.

The 15-year-old who went outside to die

Amy Hammer's 15-year-old son was diagnosed with lymphoblastic leukaemia. Chemotherapy suppressed his immune system; he developed a flesh-eating fungal infection that took his left lung. The infection spread to the right lung. Doctors gave him two days. His one wish: to go outside. Staff wheeled his bed and BiPAP machine onto the hospital grounds and added a Firefly device emitting different light wavelengths a few times a day. By day two, white count was falling. By day five, he was off BiPAP onto nasal cannula. A new CT showed 60–70% of the right-lung infection cleared. He went home. No change to medication or treatment — only the sunlight (and Firefly) was added.

Hospital ward window position

In two-bed shared rooms, the patient in the bed nearer the window is discharged faster on average. Hospitals with bigger windows score better on patient satisfaction surveys (which are tied to reimbursement in the US). Seheult is actively trying to get patients outside in three California hospitals — he says the biggest barrier is getting staff to take them.

We are the scurvy of the 21st century. Everything is inside. We avoid the sun. We avoid discomfort. We avoid extremes of temperature. We are the generation that needs the lemon and doesn't know it. — Roger Seheult
05

Vitamin D Supplementation

Seheult is not anti-supplement. He takes vitamin D himself. His position: supplement when needed, particularly through northern winters, but understand it's not a substitute for sun exposure — and in his clinical experience, much of the apparent benefit of high vitamin D blood levels is actually a marker for outdoor time rather than the molecule itself.

Vitamin D is unusual: technically a vitamin, but functionally a steroid hormone. It's lipid-soluble, which lets it pass through cell membranes and bind directly to nuclear DNA, where it acts as a transcription factor — affecting which genes get expressed. Vitamin D receptors exist on immune cells, in calcium-handling tissue and elsewhere, which is why deficiency shows up across so many systems.

DEFICIENCY~1 billion people globally are vitamin D deficient. ~50% of the world's population is insufficient. DARKER SKINMore melanin = harder to make vitamin D in skin. Need more time outside, especially at northern latitudes. UK WINTERSun is too low for UVB to penetrate atmosphere. No vitamin D production from sun for several months. Supplementation is reasonable. FOOD SOURCESMushrooms, certain fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines).
A BMJ meta-analysis of randomised trials (Martineau et al.) found daily vitamin D supplementation reduces risk of acute respiratory infections. A separate trial showed 2,000 IU/day reduced the risk of all-cause autoimmune conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn's, ulcerative colitis and others).
Vitamin D is fat-soluble. It can be overdosed. Excess affects calcium metabolism — abnormally high blood calcium. Rare, but possible. Get blood levels checked rather than self-dosing indefinitely. Especially relevant if combining high-dose supplements with significant sun exposure.

Seheult's clinical observation during COVID: hospitalised patients with normal vitamin D blood levels did far better than those with low levels — but giving low-vitamin-D patients a supplement on admission did not change outcomes meaningfully. His interpretation: vitamin D was acting as a biomarker for sun exposure, and the active protective ingredient (infrared, mitochondrial benefit) was what they were actually missing. The molecule was a flag, not the fix.

06

Red Light Therapy & Photobiomodulation

Red and near-infrared light devices (masks, panels, beds) sit at the visible end of the infrared range. Glenn Jeffery at University College London has run randomised trials on 670 nm light specifically — the wavelength is real, the mechanism is real, and the magnitude on certain endpoints is meaningful.

670 nmGlenn Jeffery's headline wavelength — visible deep red, just before infrared. Improves mitochondrial efficiency. SKINPenetrates deep into skin → activates fibroblast mitochondria → fibroblasts deposit more collagen → tighter skin. The mechanism behind red-light face masks. EYESIGHT3 minutes of 670 nm in the eye in the morning improved colour discrimination. Retina is the most mitochondria-rich tissue in the body. GLUCOSEJeffery shone red light on subjects' backs after a glucose load. Their blood sugar spike was lower. Exhaled CO₂ rose — evidence the mitochondria metabolised the glucose faster.

The 15-minute saturation point

Across fruit flies, mosquitoes, bees and humans, Jeffery has observed the same pattern: about 15 minutes of red light flips a switch in the mitochondria. More exposure beyond that point doesn't add benefit. The hypothesis sits around complex IV of the electron transport chain, but the mechanism is still being characterised. The field is called photobiomodulation.

Systemic effect from local exposure

In Jeffery's glucose study, light only on the back produced a systemic glucose-handling improvement. Mitochondria appear to communicate across cells, so local exposure can have body-wide effects. The corollary: where you put the device matters most for local cosmetic effects (face mask for facial skin), but doesn't have to cover the whole body for systemic mitochondrial benefit.

If you're already getting plenty of infrared from real sunlight, additional red-light devices add diminishing returns — Seheult uses the scurvy analogy: an extra lemon has little value if your diet is already rich in fruit and vegetables.
07

Practical Sunlight Protocol

The actionable version. Roughly 93% of Americans and 92% of Brits spend their time indoors. Closing some of that gap is the lowest-hanging intervention in the framework.

DURATION~15 minutes is the inflection point at which the mitochondrial benefit appears to saturate. Seheult aims for 15 minutes at lunchtime when ICU shifts allow. TIMING (low UV)Morning and evening — sun low in sky → less UVB (less burn risk) but still good infrared. Best for those concerned about UV damage. TIMING (max IR)Midday delivers the most infrared, but also the most UVB. Use sun-cautiously if you haven't built up tolerance. CLOUDY DAYSCloud (water vapour) absorbs much of the infrared. You still get more outside on a cloudy day than inside, but less than on a clear day. TREES VS CONCRETETrees reflect infrared (which is why satellites use IR to map forest cover). A green park gives you more infrared than a concrete urban environment. CLOTHINGUVB is mostly blocked by clothes. Infrared passes through clothes. So "covered up but outside" still delivers most of the mitochondrial benefit while reducing burn risk. WHERE ON BODYFor mitochondrial effect, location doesn't matter — exposure anywhere works systemically. For circadian effect, light must enter the eyes specifically. WINDOWSModern windows (especially in hot climates) often filter infrared. Sitting indoors near a sunny window is not a substitute for being outside.
If you're shift-bound or office-bound, 15 minutes outside at lunch is the realistic target. A walk in the park beats a walk on a city street; both beat lunch at your desk.

Two further leverages worth adding: walking outside compounds the sunlight benefit with exercise (another NEWSTART pillar) and with fresh air; and walking among trees adds the phytoncide benefit covered later. The pillars stack.

08

Circadian Rhythm — Bright Days, Dark Nights

Mitochondrial sunlight benefit happens through the body. Circadian rhythm runs through the eyes specifically. A recent paper titled "Dark days and bright nights" correlated that exact pattern — the modern norm — with increased mortality. The fix is symmetrical: maximise daytime light exposure, minimise nighttime light exposure.

SAD lamps & the 3,000 lux-hour target

SAD (seasonal affective disorder) lamps deliver about 10,000 lux at close range. Used in the morning, they entrain the circadian clock and stimulate the perihabenular nucleus — a region in the brain that, when under-stimulated by light, contributes to depression. The dose target is roughly 3,000 lux-hours: at 10,000 lux, that's about 20 minutes a day. Available cheaply on Amazon, ~$20. Position 11–16 inches from the face.

Important: this fixes the depression / circadian piece, not the mitochondrial piece. SAD lamps emit visible light only. They are not a substitute for sun exposure.

Why nighttime light is a bigger problem than people think

Two simultaneous harms when light enters the eyes at night: (1) it suppresses pineal melatonin production — losing the very antioxidant that protects mitochondria; (2) it confuses circadian rhythm, because the brain interprets light as "still daytime" and pushes the sleep-onset signal back by hours. Several days of evening screen exposure can shift natural sleep onset from 10pm to 1am.

Reading a paper book under a lamp delivers far less light to the eye than reading the same text on a Kindle. The Kindle delays sleep onset and suppresses melatonin meaningfully. Candlelight is excellent for sleep (only fire risk to manage).

Blue blockers, night modes, sleep masks

Phone "night shift" modes red-shift the spectrum but still emit enough light overall to suppress melatonin. Blue-blocker glasses help but light still bleeds in around the frames. Better than nothing, but not a substitute for turning the screen off.

Sleep masks are worthwhile because closed eyelids still let some light reach the retina. Useful in cities or hotels where blackout isn't possible.

No nightlights in bedrooms. No clock radios with LED displays. No air conditioner status lights. Bedrooms should be as dark as possible.

BESTNo light at all in the bedroom. 2ND BESTLight with no blue spectrum (warm amber lamps, candlelight). WORSTBlue-rich light (overhead LEDs, screens, cool-white bulbs).
09

Indoor Lighting — Why LEDs Are a Problem

For the entire history of human lighting — fire, candle, kerosene lamp, incandescent bulb — light came as full spectrum. Some red, some yellow, some green, some blue, all together. LEDs and fluorescent bulbs broke this pattern by design: efficiency means stripping out wavelengths the eye can't see and concentrating energy in the visible band, especially blue.

For the first time in history, humans are spending most of their waking hours under narrow-spectrum light — blue-heavy, red-poor. Evolution has no precedent for this. The retina has metabolic consequences.

The Glenn Jeffery incandescent swap (preprint)

Jeffery took 22 office workers operating under standard LED lighting and replaced their bulbs with incandescents. Result (preprint, not yet peer-reviewed): a 25% improvement in colour discrimination. Mechanism: the cones in the retina are the most metabolically active tissue in the body and the richest in mitochondria. Older mitochondria produce less energy; cones with under-fuelled mitochondria distinguish colour less well. Restore the red-spectrum input the cones evolved with and discrimination recovers.

In the US, regular incandescent bulbs (general service lamps) are now restricted by energy efficiency law. Specialty incandescents (chandelier, microwave, oven) are still available. UK regulations are similar. Realistic options: full-spectrum LED bulbs, halogen bulbs, or specialty incandescents.

Practical defaults: get outside whenever possible during daylight; SAD lamp in the morning if dark winter mornings keep you indoors; warm-spectrum lighting only after sunset; bedroom completely dark.

Water — Sauna, Cold & Interferon

The "W" in NEWSTART. Beyond drinking, the case is for hydrotherapy — using water externally to manipulate body temperature, which in turn manipulates the innate immune system. The lever is interferon.

Innate vs adaptive immunity (and why fever matters)

The adaptive immune system makes specific antibodies — the COVID conversation everyone became familiar with. The innate immune system is the older, faster, less specific first line: monocytes, natural killer cells, and a master signalling molecule called interferon.

Interferon is so effective against viral infection that essentially every virus that successfully infects humans has evolved a workaround for it. Critical fact: interferon production goes up sharply with body temperature. Mouse studies (mice run the same body temperature as humans) show that going from 37°C to 38°C — just below fever — triggers a roughly 10-fold increase in interferon production. Lymphocytes pulled from the body and warmed to 38–39°C in vitro show the same off-the-charts interferon spike.

The take-home Seheult draws from this: don't routinely suppress fevers with paracetamol/acetaminophen unless they're high enough to cause complications (cardiac strain, seizures, dehydration). The reflex of treating fever to feel better is, in his framing, cutting the legs out from under the immune response. Note this contradicts the most common over-the-counter advice — judgement call.

Sauna as artificial fever

A sauna heats the body through water vapour's high specific heat capacity. The body temperature rise creates the same interferon-stimulating environment a fever would. Finnish data (a country with more saunas than people) shows a clear dose-response: 4–7 sauna sessions per week is associated with substantially lower cardiovascular mortality than the once-a-week baseline. Studies link COVID severity inversely to interferon levels — high interferon, mild COVID; low interferon, severe COVID.

Why the cold finish?

A brief cold exposure (~1 minute) at the end of a sauna session does two things, per Seheult's reading of older hydrotherapy literature:

Vasoconstriction → heat retention. Cold contracts superficial blood vessels, so when you step out of the sauna, you radiate less heat. Counter-intuitive but useful: a brief cold finish keeps core temperature elevated for longer.

Demargination. White blood cells normally adhere to the inner walls of blood vessels. When vessels constrict suddenly, those cells get knocked off into circulation, where they go off and do their immune work. The protocol: long heat exposure (~20 min sauna) followed by a brief cold finish (~1 min cold shower or plunge).

Air — Phytoncides, CO₂ & Forest Bathing

Air isn't just oxygen and nitrogen with no contaminants. The right air is biologically alive, carrying compounds that interact directly with the immune system. The corollary: indoor air, even if clean, is missing something the outdoor version has.

PHYTONCIDESAromatic compounds that trees release. Inhaled, they elevate natural killer cell counts and the lytic enzyme activity of those cells. JAPANESE STUDIESCEOs taken from urban offices into hinoki cypress forests showed measurable increases in NK cell number and function. The benefit lasted ~7 days. Just one day a week outside is enough to capture much of it. URBAN VERSIONWhen the same subjects were brought back to city hotels and exposed to infused phytoncide compounds, similar effects were observed.

Indoor CO₂ and cognitive performance

Joseph Allen at the Harvard School of Public Health has demonstrated that elevated indoor CO₂ — common in sealed offices, conference rooms, hotel rooms and cars on recirculated air — measurably impairs cognitive performance. The fix is trivial: cracking a window meaningfully drops CO₂ levels. Cheap CO₂ meters are available on Amazon to monitor.

Practical: open windows when you can. Switch car ventilation off recirculation. In hotels, prefer rooms with windows that open. If you spend hours in a small room (studio, office), a CO₂ meter on the desk gives you real-time feedback on when you need to ventilate.

Rest, Trust & Melatonin Supplementation

The closing pillars. Rest is not just nightly sleep but a weekly rhythm — a day off the phone and emails. Trust is faith-related, presented carefully as association rather than causation.

Sleep & the food-choice effect

As a sleep physician, Seheult notes one specific finding worth flagging: people who don't get enough sleep gravitate to higher-carbohydrate food choices. This is one of the proposed mechanisms by which insufficient sleep contributes to weight gain — not directly through metabolism, but through what you reach for.

Weekly rest

Beyond daily sleep, Seheult argues for a weekly rest — a whole day off devices, off email, doing things outside normal work mode. The argument is structural rather than purely physiological: without an enforced break, the modern default is no break.

Trust — faith as a stress buffer

A growing body of research links religious faith — across denominations — to lower depression, lower anxiety and better stress tolerance. Seheult is careful to call this association, not causation: people with faith may have more friends, stronger community ties, more social support, and these may do most of the work.

A specific study by Krause (University of Texas) divided people into conditional forgivers (will forgive if the other person apologises) and unconditional forgivers (will forgive regardless). Unconditional forgivers had less depression, less anxiety, and fewer somatic symptoms. The strongest predictor of which category someone fell into — odds ratio ~2.5 — was the answer to one question: do you believe that God has forgiven you?

A separate caveat: faith is only protective when the relationship is positive. Believing in a vindictive or punishing god is associated with worse outcomes, not better.

Randomised controlled trials of cognitive behavioural therapy show CBT becomes more effective when faith elements are integrated for patients who hold that faith. Seheult is explicit that these elements should never be imposed on patients who don't want them.

Melatonin supplementation — when it's useful

Seheult's specific positions on swallowed melatonin:

  • Useful for occasional difficulty falling asleep — small dose, no more than 5 mg.
  • Useful for jet lag and for advancing a delayed circadian rhythm forward.
  • Useful for certain specific sleep disorders.
  • Not recommended as a routine, large-dose nightly supplement.

Side effect of high-dose melatonin: increased mental irritability — things set you off more easily. There is also concern (unproven) that chronic high-dose supplementation may suppress the pineal gland's own melatonin production over time. Evidence isn't yet there to confirm or rule this out.

13

Key Takeaways — The Actionable Set

Distilled to things you can change tomorrow.

  • 15 minutes outside daily. Lunchtime if you're in an office. Cloudy is still better than indoors. Mornings and evenings if UV exposure worries you.
  • Walk under trees, not concrete. Trees reflect infrared and emit phytoncides. A park beats a pavement.
  • Sit by the window in the office, not at the back. But know that modern windows filter most infrared — outside is still better.
  • Vitamin D supplement through northern winter (UK November–March). 2,000 IU/day is a reasonable starting dose. Get blood levels checked before going higher.
  • Sauna 3–4× weekly if you have access. End with a brief (~1 min) cold shower. Heat duration matters more than the cold.
  • Bedroom dark, completely dark. Sleep mask if you can't control external light. No clock LEDs, no nightlights.
  • Phone/Kindle off ~1 hour before sleep. Or paper book under a warm lamp. Candlelight is fine.
  • SAD lamp 20 minutes in the morning if you're at northern latitude in winter — fixes mood and circadian alignment, but is not a sun substitute for mitochondrial benefit.
  • Crack a window in offices, hotels and cars. Indoor CO₂ matters for cognition.
  • One day a week off devices. The "weekly rest" pillar.
The framework's core claim: most of the modern chronic disease load — dementia, cardiovascular, diabetes, kidney — traces back to mitochondrial dysfunction, and most of the interventions that reverse mitochondrial dysfunction (sun, heat, exercise, sleep, low oxidative load) are free and require no prescription. The barrier is behavioural, not pharmaceutical.
